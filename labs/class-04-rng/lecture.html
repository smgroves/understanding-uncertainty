Learning From Data & Random Numbers

Parameters, estimators, and the reproducible randomness that lets you test them
Understanding Uncertainty · lecture notes, digitized

This lecture has two halves. The first asks a deceptively simple question — why do we compute the sample mean at all? — and answers it with calculus: it is the single number that minimizes mean squared error against your data, and it happens to be an unbiased estimator of the population mean with variance that shrinks like \(1/n\). The second half is about the tool that makes every experiment in this course testable: a random number generator you can seed, reproduce, and — when you write it the wrong way — wait ten times longer than you needed to.

Part 1 · Learning From Data

Generating processes and samples

Out in the world, probability spaces \((\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{E}, p)\) are happening whether or not anyone measures them. When we measure a feature \(X\) of one of those processes, we create an observation \(x_i\) of that random variable. Collect a set of observations together and you have a random sample of data. We estimate or fit models to that sample to learn about the underlying probability space and the random variable built on it — so that we can make better decisions.

Models

The real world — \((\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{E}, p)\) and the random variables built on it — is typically too difficult to understand on its own terms. This is why randomized controlled trials exist: nobody can predict how a novel surgical procedure or drug will affect the body without real evidence, because the underlying process is too complex to reason about directly. We build abstractions of the real world called models to help us understand it anyway.

Parameter and statistic

A parameter is any function of the random variable and the probability space — for example, \(\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_x p(x)\,x\) is a fixed number describing the population, whether or not anyone ever measures it. A statistic is any function of the data — for example, the sample mean \(m(X) = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i\), usually written \(\bar{X}_n\). An estimate is a statistic intended to measure the true value of a parameter: we suspect that \(\bar{X}_n\) is a "good" estimate of \(\mathbb{E}[X]\). The rest of this half of the lecture is about why.

Learning from data

Sample the process \(X\) to get data \([x_1, \dots, x_n]\), then find a loss function that generates a statistic, \(\bar{X}_n\). We suspect the sample mean is connected to the expectation \(\mathbb{E}[X]\), and we want probability to make that connection precise by answering two questions: what is the relationship between \(\bar{X}_n\) and \(\mathbb{E}[X]\), and between \(\mathbb{V}[\bar{X}_n]\) and \(\mathbb{V}[X]\)?

Why the sample mean?

First: why care about \(\bar{X}_n\) at all? Imagine predicting the next outcome of \(X\) and wanting to minimize your error — specifically, the mean squared error against the sample you already have:

$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_i (X_i - \hat{x})^2$$

The best predictor \(\hat{x}\) sets the derivative of that expression, with respect to \(\hat{x}\), to zero:

$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n 2(X_i - \hat{x}) = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \hat{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$$

That is the sample mean: the best predictor of \(X\), under mean squared error, given the sample available. The average loss at that optimum is the sample variance:

$$s^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \left(X_i - \bar{X}_n\right)^2$$

This is the prototype analysis behind essentially all of machine learning: pick a loss function, minimize it over your data, and whatever number falls out is your estimator.

Independent and identically distributed

A random sample is a collection of \(n\) random vectors, \([X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n]\). It is independent if the observed value of \(X_i\) has no influence on the observed value of \(X_j\) for \(i \neq j\), and identically distributed if every \(X_i\) is generated by the same probability space and random variable. A sample that is both is called iid. Later in the course you will handle sequences whose values are correlated in time or space, or whose generating process shifts with context — but iid is the assumption underneath everything in this lecture.

Expectation of the sample mean

Suppose \((X_1, \dots, X_n)\) is iid. What value do we expect the sample mean to take?

$$\begin{alignat*}{2}
\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n] &=& \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right] \quad &(\text{Definition})\\
&=& \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i] \quad &(\text{Linearity of } \mathbb{E})\\
&=& \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X] \quad &(\text{IID property})\\
&=& \mathbb{E}[X] \quad &(\text{Sum of } \mathbb{E}[X] \text{ taken } n \text{ times, over } n)
\end{alignat*}$$

The expectation of the sample mean is exactly the true parameter, \(\mathbb{E}[X]\). This means \(\bar{X}_n\) is an unbiased estimator of the true expected value: computing the statistic \(\bar{X}_n\) estimates the population parameter \(\mathbb{E}[X]\) without systematic error in either direction.

Variance of the sample mean

Now the harder question: the variance of the sample mean.

$$\mathbb{V}[\bar{X}_n] = \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i - \mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n]\right)^2\right]$$

Before the derivation, the answer: \(\mathbb{V}[\bar{X}_n] = \mathbb{V}[X]/n\) — the underlying population variance, divided by sample size. As \(n\) grows, the variance falls: larger samples deliver more precise estimates, in expectation. The rest of this section is the "grueling part," in the source material's own words — working out exactly why.

Variance is the mean of the square minus the square of the mean, and since \(\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n] = \mathbb{E}[X]\):

$$\mathbb{V}[\bar{X}_n] = \mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n^2] - (\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n])^2 = \mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n^2] - \mathbb{E}[X]^2$$

The problem term is \(\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n^2]\). Expanding the square of the sum produces \(n^2\) cross-terms \(X_i X_j\); because the sample is iid, \(\mathbb{E}[X_i X_j] = \mathbb{E}[X_i]\,\mathbb{E}[X_j] = \mathbb{E}[X]^2\) whenever \(i \neq j\):

$$\begin{alignat*}{2}
\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n^2] &=& \frac{1}{n^2}\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 + \sum_{i \neq j} X_i X_j\right] \quad &(\text{FOIL})\\
&=& \frac{1}{n^2}\left(\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i^2] + \sum_{i \neq j} \mathbb{E}[X_i X_j]\right) \quad &(\text{Linearity})\\
&=& \frac{1}{n^2}\left(\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i^2] + \sum_{i \neq j} \mathbb{E}[X]^2\right) \quad &(\text{IID})
\end{alignat*}$$

Among the \(n \times n\) pairs of indices, exactly \(n\) have \(i = j\) and \(n^2 - n\) have \(i \neq j\):

$$\begin{alignat*}{2}
\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n^2] &=& \frac{1}{n^2}\left(\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i^2] + (n^2 - n)\,\mathbb{E}[X]^2\right) \quad &(\text{Replace } \textstyle\sum_{i\neq j} \text{ with } n^2 - n)\\
&=& \frac{1}{n^2}\left(n\,\mathbb{E}[X^2] - n\,\mathbb{E}[X]^2\right) + \mathbb{E}[X]^2 \quad &(X_i \text{ are IID})\\
&=& \frac{1}{n}\Big(\underbrace{\mathbb{E}[X^2] - \mathbb{E}[X]^2}_{\mathbb{V}[X]}\Big) + \mathbb{E}[X]^2 \quad &(\text{Simplify})\\
&=& \frac{\mathbb{V}[X]}{n} + \mathbb{E}[X]^2 \quad &(\mathbb{V}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - \mathbb{E}[X]^2)
\end{alignat*}$$

Plug that back into \(\mathbb{V}[\bar{X}_n] = \mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n^2] - \mathbb{E}[X]^2\):

$$\begin{alignat*}{2}
\mathbb{V}[\bar{X}_n] &=& \mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n^2] - \mathbb{E}[X]^2\\
&=& \frac{\mathbb{V}[X]}{n} + \mu^2 - \mu^2\\
\mathbb{V}[\bar{X}_n] &=& \frac{\mathbb{V}[X]}{n}
\end{alignat*}$$

The variance of the sample mean is the variance of the underlying variable, divided by \(n\) — exactly the claim from the start of this section, now derived rather than asserted.

Behavior of the estimator

Putting the two results together:

$$\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n] = \mathbb{E}[X], \qquad \mathbb{V}[\bar{X}_n] = \frac{\mathbb{V}[X]}{n}$$

\(\bar{X}_n\) is an unbiased estimator of \(\mathbb{E}[X]\), since its expectation equals the parameter exactly. And because its variance falls as \(n\) grows, the uncertainty about the estimate itself shrinks with more data — the two properties together are why the sample mean is trustworthy, not just conventional.

What is learning?

Four steps summarize the whole prototype:

  1. Understand your phenomenon of interest — the probability space \((\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{E}, p)\) and the random variable \(X\) built on it. What are you measuring, why, and what variables determine outcomes?
  2. Sample randomly from the process to gather data.
  3. Find a loss function that aligns your model's estimates with the true parameters (sum of squared error, sample mean).
  4. Analyze how the model behaves as a function of the data — does it provide good estimates and predictions? (The sample mean is unbiased; the sample variance shrinks to zero at a rate of \(1/n\).)

The rest of machine learning is, in this framing, just making the model more complicated and learning to recognize what kinds of problems arise when you do.

Part 2 · Random Numbers

Uncertainty is at the core of probability: how do we quantify and manage risk? The paradox is that we want a consistent answer to emerge from randomized experiments, so that our conclusions don't depend on chance. We often want to test ideas against randomness we can control and manipulate — NumPy gives us the tools to generate random vectors (a sample), and this course uses them constantly.

Random numbers aren't random

Computers are deterministic machines and cannot create true randomness on their own. A pseudorandom number generator starts from a seed value and performs calculations that produce a sequence of numbers with no detectable correlation from value to value. Your analysis's conclusions should never depend on which seed you happened to pick — but fixing the seed is exactly what makes debugging and reproducibility possible: the same seed always regenerates the same sequence.

Generating random numbers

To create a stream of random numbers: rng = np.random.default_rng(seed=100). Each draw from the stream is then a method call on rng:

MethodUse
rng.integers(low, high, size=N)Generate a random integer between low and high - 1
rng.choice(A, size=N)Select \(N\) random elements from the set \(A\)
rng.choice(A, size=N, replace=False)Select \(N\) random elements from \(A\), without replacing elements between draws
rng.random(size=N)Select \(N\) uniformly distributed numbers between 0 and 1
 
# Example
rng = np.random.default_rng(seed=100)  # Create a random number stream
rng.integers(0, 5, size=10)
array([3, 4, 0, 2, 0, 1, 2, 0, 2, 4])

Example: rolling a die

To get acquainted with .choice: imagine rolling a six-sided die \(K = 100\) times and averaging the result, then repeating that whole experiment \(B = 50{,}000\) times. We already know \(\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n] = \mathbb{E}[X]\) and \(\mathbb{V}[\bar{X}_n] = \mathbb{V}[X]/n\) from Part 1 — in principle we could grind out the formulas here, but it is faster to let randomization simulate the experiment directly.

rng = np.random.default_rng(seed=100)  # Create a random number stream

die = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]
K = 100
B = 50_000
result = []
for i in range(B):
    R = rng.choice(die, size=K, replace=True)
    result.append(R)
result = np.array(result)
sns.histplot(result.mean(axis=1), bins=80)
print('Sample mean: ', x_bar := np.mean(result.mean(axis=1)))
print('Sample variance: ', s_sq := np.mean((result.mean(axis=1) - x_bar) ** 2, axis=0))
Sample mean:  3.4998618
Sample variance:  0.029393562900760003

Despite the die having only six outcomes, the 50,000 averages form a smooth bell shape tightly clustered around 3.5 — not the ragged six-bar histogram of a single roll. That number is not a coincidence: \(\mathbb{E}[X] = (1+2+3+4+5+6)/6 = 21/6 = 3.5\), matching the sample mean above to four decimal places. Standardize those same averages — subtract \(\mu = 3.5\) and divide by the standard error \(\sigma/\sqrt{K}\), where \(\sigma = \sqrt{35/6}\) is the die's own standard deviation — and the resulting z-scores spread out over roughly \([-2.6, 4.2]\) in a shape the source material titles "Foreshadowing": the full explanation of why that bell shape is universal is the Central Limit Theorem, built from scratch in the Class 11 lecture.

Example: choosing from a grid

Build a grid of \(K\) evenly spaced points between 0 and 1, \(\{1/K, 2/K, \dots, K/K\}\) (np.linspace(1/K, 1, K)), and draw from it at random. The expectation of a single draw is:

$$\frac{1}{K}\cdot\frac{1}{K} + \frac{1}{K}\cdot\frac{2}{K} + \cdots + \frac{1}{K}\cdot\frac{K}{K} = \sum_{k=1}^K \frac{1}{K}\cdot\frac{k}{K} = \frac{K(K+1)}{2K^2} = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2K}$$

Three things fall out of that formula as \(K\) grows large: each individual grid value has probability \(1/K \to 0\), so every specific value becomes "impossible" to land on exactly; the average approaches \(1/2\); and for any \(0 < a < b < 1\), the probability of landing between \(a\) and \(b\) approaches \(b - a\) — a discrete grid, made fine enough, behaves like a continuous uniform distribution.

K = 10
X = np.linspace(1, K, K) / K
print(rng.choice(X, size=5, replace=True))

K = 10000
X = np.linspace(1, K, K) / K
rng.choice(X, size=5, replace=True)
[0.7 0.9 0.9 0.8 0.2]
array([0.3972, 0.0014, 0.6256, 0.6294, 0.503 ])

Logical statements and looping an experiment

Comparisons on an array of draws produce an array of booleans, which cast to 0/1 the same way anywhere else in Python:

R = rng.random(5)
I = ((R >= .25) * (R < .75)).astype(int)
print(I)
[1 1 1 0 0]

Simulation is the best available way to understand a random (stochastic) phenomenon whose exact formula is inconvenient or impossible to derive by hand: loop the experiment, collect the statistic every time, and look at what comes out.

B = 10_000
N = 100

results = []
for b in range(B):
    sample_b = rng.random(N)
    results.append(sample_b.mean())
loop_results = np.array(results)

print(loop_results)
[0.49540872 0.51480983 0.51894682 ... 0.51209575 0.50678215 0.49174426]

Writing simulations for speed

Simulations can be expensive, and how you write the loop changes how long it takes to run — its "wall time." Four ways to write the same computation, from easiest-to-write to fastest-to-run:

  1. Simple loops. Code compiles step by step in the interpreter, so it is easy to write but inefficient.
  2. List comprehension. The instructions are given once, so the interpreter can optimize a little: results = np.array([rng.random(N).mean() for b in range(B)]).
  3. Vectorized NumPy. NumPy calls pre-compiled code written in C: results = rng.random(size=(N, B)).mean(axis=0).
  4. Compiled loops (Numba). Pre-compile the Python into machine code directly with the @njit decorator, then call it like an ordinary function.
# The `time` library
# Simple Loop
start = time.perf_counter()
results = []
for b in range(B):
    R = rng.random(N)
    results.append(R.mean())
loop_results = np.array(results)
end = time.perf_counter()
print(f"Naive loop time: {end - start:.5f} seconds")

# List Comprehension
start = time.perf_counter()
results = np.array([rng.random(N).mean() for b in range(B)])
end = time.perf_counter()
print(f"List comprehension time: {end - start:.5f} seconds")

# Vectorized Numpy
start = time.perf_counter()
results = rng.random(size=(B, N)).mean(axis=1)
end = time.perf_counter()
print(f"Vectorized time: {end - start:.5f} seconds")

# Numba-accelerated loop
from numba import njit
@njit
def numba_loop(B, N):
    results = np.empty(B)
    for b in range(B):
        total = 0.0
        for i in range(N):
            total += np.random.random()
        results[b] = total / N
    return results

numba_loop(B, N)  # Run once to compile

start = time.perf_counter()
results = numba_loop(B, N)
end = time.perf_counter()
print(f"Compiled Numba time: {end - start:.5f} seconds")
Naive loop time: 0.04156 seconds
List comprehension time: 0.03743 seconds
Vectorized time: 0.00426 seconds
Compiled Numba time: 0.00272 seconds

Experimental results

On this run, the naive loop and the list comprehension are close to each other (0.042s vs. 0.037s — list comprehension is "more Pythonic" but barely faster), vectorized NumPy is roughly an order of magnitude faster than either (0.0043s), and compiled Numba edges out even the vectorized version (0.0027s) despite having "no right" to outperform NumPy's own optimized C, in the source material's words. The exact numbers depend on the machine — RAM, CPU, and whatever else is running — but the ranking is typical. The practical lesson: write code that works first, then optimize. As Knuth put it, premature optimization is the root of all evil.

The same standardizing trick from the die example reappears here: z_scores = (results - mu) / sem with mu = 0.5 and sigma = sqrt(1/12) (the mean and standard deviation of a continuous Uniform(0,1)) turns the histogram of sample means into another "Foreshadowing" bell curve — the same Central Limit Theorem the die example previewed, now for a different population entirely.

FAQ

If the sample mean is unbiased, is it the only reasonable estimator?

No — unbiasedness is one desirable property, not the only one. The median is also a reasonable estimator of central tendency (unbiased for a symmetric population, and far more robust to outliers), and later in the course you will meet estimators that trade a little bias for a large reduction in variance. Mean squared error minimization is what singles out the sample mean specifically; a different loss function singles out a different statistic.

Why does fixing a seed not compromise the randomness of an experiment?

Because the seed only controls which sequence of pseudorandom numbers you draw, not whether the sequence has the statistical properties (uniformity, lack of correlation) you need. Two different seeds produce two different, equally valid random sequences; a fixed seed just lets you and anyone reading your code regenerate the exact same one, which is what reproducibility requires.

Does vectorized code always beat a loop?

Not automatically, but almost always for numeric array work, because the loop over elements happens inside pre-compiled C rather than inside the Python interpreter. The exception is when the "vectorized" version has to build a much larger intermediate array than the loop ever holds in memory — at that point memory bandwidth can erase the advantage. Measure before assuming.
