The CDF, and Sampling by Inverting It

One staircase answers two opposite questions
Understanding Uncertainty · a build-it-from-scratch walkthrough

A histogram tells you how the data clusters. The cumulative distribution function asks a sharper question — "what fraction of the sample is at or below this value?" — and its answer is a single climbing staircase. Read that staircase left-to-right and you estimate a probability; read it backwards and you can conjure brand-new samples from any distribution, using nothing but a coin-flip's worth of randomness.

Built on the Class 06 notebooks on the empirical CDF and the inverse probability transform. Data: age_at_diagnosis (years) — a random sample of 100 patients from the METABRIC breast-cancer cohort (Curtis et al., Nature 2012), shipped as data.json.

The ECDF staircase

The recipe is exactly the count you just described. For each value \(x\), count how many observations are at or below it, then divide by \(n\). That proportion is the empirical CDF:

$$\hat{F}(x) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbb{I}\{\,x_i \le x\,\}$$

The summand is an indicator: it is 1 when \(x_i \le x\) and 0 otherwise, so the sum is just a count. As \(x\) sweeps rightward the count can only grow, which is why \(\hat{F}\) is a monotone staircase: flat between observations, then a jump of \(1/n\) at each one. It starts at 0 to the left of the smallest value and reaches 1 at the largest.

In code the whole estimator is one line — loop, compare, divide:

def ecdf_at(x, X):
    return sum(1 for xi in X if xi <= x) / len(X)   # proportion at or below x
Check your understanding

In the builder above, drag the query age all the way to the right edge. Why does \(\hat{F}(x)\) flatten out at exactly 1.00 and refuse to climb higher?

Show answer

Once \(x\) passes the oldest patient in the sample, every observation satisfies \(x_i \le x\), so the count is \(n\) and \(\hat{F}(x) = n/n = 1\). A CDF is a probability, and no more than all of the data can sit below a threshold — the staircase is capped at 1 by construction.

What the staircase estimates

The ECDF is an estimate. Behind it sits the true CDF \(F(x) = P(X \le x)\) of the population the patients were drawn from — a smooth, non-decreasing curve running from 0 up to 1. The staircase is what one finite, iid sample lets you see of it.

The reassuring fact is that the estimate improves with data. Slide the sample size up and watch the staircase's steps shrink and press in against the smooth reference curve — with more observations, \(\hat{F} \to F\) everywhere at once.

Why the CDF, not the density

You can build \(\hat{F}\) with nothing but counting — no bandwidth, no bins, no smoothing choice to defend. That is what makes the CDF the safest first summary of a distribution, and the natural object to invert when you want to generate data rather than describe it.

Reading it backwards

What do you get, and why does the fact that the staircase never decreases guarantee this backwards lookup always has an answer?
I've done the Think step — reveal Pair & Share
  • Pair · 3 minCompare with a neighbour: at \(u = 0.5\), what is the age you land on called?

Running the staircase backwards is the quantile function, the inverse CDF \(F^{-1}\). You pick a height \(u \in [0,1]\), slide right until you hit the staircase, and drop down to the age underneath. Because the CDF is monotone, every height has a well-defined landing point — the smallest observed value whose ECDF is at least \(u\):

$$\hat{F}^{-1}(u) = \inf\{\,x : \hat{F}(x) \ge u\,\} = x_{(\lceil n u \rceil)}$$

Here \(x_{(1)} \le x_{(2)} \le \dots \le x_{(n)}\) are the sorted ages (the order statistics), and \(\lceil n u \rceil\) is the rank you round up to. At \(u = 0.5\) the landing point is the median. Drive the height slider and watch the green mapping: y-axis, across to the staircase, down to an age.

Sampling by inverting

Think · Pair · Share · uniform in, data out
  • Think · 2 minSuppose you feed a stream of uniform heights — evenly scattered between 0 and 1 — into that backwards lookup, one after another. Where do the resulting ages pile up? Predict which age ranges collect the most landings, and connect that to how steeply the staircase rises there.
I've done the Think step — reveal Pair & Share
  • Pair · 3 minCompare: a steep part of the staircase covers a lot of height in a little age — so does it catch many uniforms or few?
  • Share · 2 minPredict the shape of the histogram of landed ages, then draw 200 and check.

Here is the payoff, the inverse probability transform. If \(U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)\) then \(F^{-1}(U)\) has distribution \(F\). So to draw from any distribution whose CDF you can invert, you need just one uniform random number and one backwards lookup:

$$U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1) \quad\Longrightarrow\quad F^{-1}(U) \sim F$$

The intuition is exactly your prediction above. A uniform height is equally likely to land anywhere on the y-axis. Where the staircase rises steeply, a tall band of heights maps into a narrow band of ages, so those ages collect many landings — high density. Where it is flat, few heights map there — low density. The inverse hands the probability mass right back where the data put it.

With only a sample, you invert the ECDF instead of the true \(F\). Because each of the \(n\) order statistics is equally likely to be selected, \(\hat{F}^{-1}(U)\) draws the observed ages back with replacement — which is precisely the bootstrap. Press "Draw 200" and watch the sampled histogram (solid) grow into the shape of the original data (dashed).

Draw uniforms, invert, and watch the distribution reappear
Top: each draw is a uniform height mapped through the staircase down to an age. Bottom: those ages accumulate into a histogram. It converges to the dashed outline of the original 100 ages — you sampled the distribution back out of its own CDF.
Because the ECDF is a step function, its inverse can only return values the data actually contained — so inverse-transforming the ECDF is resampling. Inverting a smooth CDF instead fills in the gaps and yields genuinely new values.

FAQ

Why does the quantile jump instead of moving smoothly?

The ECDF is a step function, so its inverse can only return the specific values the sample contains. Sliding \(u\) a little often lands on the same order statistic until you cross the next step. Inverting a smooth CDF (like the Normal reference) instead gives a continuous quantile that returns values between the observed ones.

Is inverse-transforming the ECDF really the same as the bootstrap?

Yes. Every order statistic is equally likely to be selected, so drawing \(\hat{F}^{-1}(U)\) repeatedly is sampling the observed values uniformly with replacement — the textbook nonparametric bootstrap. The connection is exact, not an analogy.

Why round the rank up with \(\lceil n u\rceil\) instead of down?

The inverse we want is \(\inf\{x : \hat{F}(x) \ge u\}\) — the first age at which the staircase reaches height \(u\). Rounding up picks that first order statistic. Rounding down (or interpolating) defines a different, equally valid quantile convention; this lab fixes one so the autograder has a single right answer.

What if two patients have the same age?

Then the staircase takes a double-height jump of \(2/n\) at that value, and both order statistics equal it. The formulas are unchanged — ties just stack the steps.
