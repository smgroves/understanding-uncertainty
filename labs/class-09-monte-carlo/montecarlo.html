Monte Carlo & the Law of Large Numbers

Why averaging random draws answers questions you cannot integrate by hand
Understanding Uncertainty · a build-it-from-scratch walkthrough

You want the average price of a used car, or the value of an integral, or the number \(\pi\) — and you cannot or would rather not compute it exactly. The Monte Carlo recipe is the same every time: draw random numbers, apply a simple rule to each, and average. The weak law of large numbers is the promise that this average settles on the true answer, and it even tells you how fast.

Built on the Class 09 notebooks 08_1_wlln (weak law of large numbers, via Chebyshev) and 08_2_monte_carlo (Monte Carlo for a function, a region, and \(\pi\)). Data: 92 used-car listings scraped from Charlottesville Craigslist (cville_cars.csv), prices in thousands of dollars.

The weak law of large numbers

Take \(n\) draws \(X_1, \dots, X_n\) that are iid — same distribution, drawn independently — and average them into the sample mean \(\bar{X}_n\). Two facts about that average are exact, and together they explain the pinching ribbon.

First, the sample mean is right on average: its expected value is exactly the quantity you are chasing.

$$\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n] = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right] = \mu$$

Second, and this is the engine, the sample mean's variance shrinks as you add draws. Averaging \(n\) independent copies divides the variance by \(n\):

$$\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \operatorname{sd}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$

That standard deviation of the estimate has its own name — the standard error — and its \(1/\sqrt{n}\) shape is exactly the ribbon you saw narrow. Chebyshev's inequality turns the shrinking variance into a probability statement: the chance the average is more than a distance \(d\) from the truth cannot exceed

$$\Pr\!\left(\,\lvert \bar{X}_n - \mu \rvert \geq d\,\right) \;\leq\; \frac{\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X}_n)}{d^2} \;=\; \frac{\sigma^2}{n\,d^2}$$

Read the right-hand side: for any fixed tolerance \(d\), the bound goes to zero as \(n \to \infty\). That is the weak law of large numbers — the sample mean converges in probability to \(\mu\). The average is not guaranteed to be close on any single run, but the probability of being far away is squeezed to nothing.

The one number to remember

The error of an average of \(n\) iid draws scales like \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\). Want one more digit of accuracy — cut the error by ten? You need one hundred times the draws. That single fact governs every Monte Carlo estimate in this lab.

Averaging a function over draws

Nothing in the weak law required \(X\) itself. Apply any fixed rule \(g\) to each draw first, and the same argument makes the running average of \(g(X_i)\) converge to the expected value of \(g\). This is the whole Monte Carlo method in one line:

$$\bar{y}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n g(X_i) \;\xrightarrow[\;n\to\infty\;]{\;\text{pr}\;}\; \mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int g(x)\, f(x)\, dx$$

The left side is arithmetic a computer does instantly; the right side is an integral you might have no way to evaluate by hand. Monte Carlo trades the integral for a coin-flipping loop, and the weak law guarantees the trade is fair.

Note · a tempting mistake

It is tempting to fold the density back in yourself and compute \(\sum_i g(x_i)\,f(x_i)\) instead. Resist it. The draws \(X_i\) already arrive with frequency proportional to \(f\) — that is what "sampled from \(F\)" means — so multiplying by \(f(x_i)\) again double-counts the density. The correct estimator is the plain average \(\frac1n\sum_i g(x_i)\), with no density evaluation and no grid spacing anywhere in it. The sample already did that work for you.

The special case that unlocks geometry is the indicator rule. Let \(g\) be 1 when a point lands in a region \(A\) and 0 otherwise. Then the expected value of \(g\) is just the probability of landing in \(A\), which for uniform draws is the area of \(A\):

$$\bar{p}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{I}\{X_i \in A\} \;\xrightarrow[\;n\to\infty\;]{\;\text{pr}\;}\; \Pr(X \in A) = \operatorname{area}(A)$$

So "count the fraction of random points that land inside a shape" is a legitimate way to measure that shape's area. The next section turns that fraction into \(\pi\).

The quarter disc inside the unit square has area \(\pi/4\), so by the indicator rule the fraction of uniform points landing inside converges to \(\pi/4\). Multiply by four and you have a \(\pi\) machine that uses nothing but a seeded random number generator:

$$\hat{\pi}_n = 4 \cdot \frac{\#\{\,i : x_i^2 + y_i^2 < 1\,\}}{n}$$

Add darts below and watch the estimate crawl toward 3.14159. It gets there — but slowly, and that slowness is the point of the final section.

Show answer

Going from 100 to 10,000 darts is a 100-fold increase in \(n\), but the error shrinks like \(1/\sqrt{n}\), so it improves by \(\sqrt{100} = 10\)-fold, not 100-fold. Ten times the accuracy costs a hundred times the darts. This is the same \(1/\sqrt{n}\) tax the standard-error band charged in the first widget.

The weak law promises convergence; the standard error \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\) sets the speed. On ordinary axes that \(1/\sqrt{n}\) decay is a lazy-looking curve, but on log–log axes it straightens into a line whose slope is the exponent. A slope of \(-\tfrac12\) is the visual signature of Monte Carlo.

Note · slow, but dimension-proof

\(1/\sqrt{n}\) is genuinely slow next to a good deterministic quadrature rule in one dimension. Monte Carlo earns its keep in high dimensions: its error stays \(1/\sqrt{n}\) whether you are integrating over a line or over a thousand-dimensional space, while grid-based rules collapse under the curse of dimensionality. Slow but flat beats fast but exploding.

FAQ

Is Monte Carlo just "guessing"?

No — it is averaging, and the weak law of large numbers guarantees the average converges to the exact answer. What is random is any single run; what is reliable is the long-run average and the \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\) rate at which it settles.

Why resample the 92 prices with replacement instead of just averaging them?

Averaging all 92 once gives the population mean exactly; there is no Monte Carlo in that. Resampling with replacement lets you watch an estimate built from random draws converge to that known truth — the point of the lab is the convergence, and knowing the true mean lets you measure the error.

Why must I use the provided make_rng instead of Python's random?

Reproducibility. A seeded generator produces the same stream every run, so the grader (and you) can check that a given seed yields a fixed answer. It also matches the browser widgets and both samplers exactly, so the same seed tells the same story everywhere.

Does the estimate ever stop improving?

In theory it keeps improving as \(1/\sqrt{n}\) forever. In practice floating-point precision and the quality of the random generator set a floor, but you will hit your patience long before you hit that floor — a hundredfold more draws buys only one more digit.
