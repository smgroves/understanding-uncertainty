The Weak Law of Large Numbers & Monte Carlo

A bound on how far a sample mean can wander, and the computational trick it unlocks
Understanding Uncertainty · lecture notes, digitized

Chebyshev's inequality turns a shrinking variance into an actual bound on how far a sample mean can stray from the truth — the weak law of large numbers, made precise. That single guarantee is also, in disguise, a general-purpose computational tool: draw random numbers, apply a rule, average, and you have just estimated an integral you might have had no way to evaluate directly. That trick is Monte Carlo, and Part 2 is entirely about using it.

Part 1 · The Weak Law of Large Numbers

Stochastic distance

The prototype of distance is absolute value:

$$|x-y| = \sqrt{(x-y)^2} = \begin{cases} x-y, & x\gt y \\ 0, & x=y \\ y-x, & y\gt x \end{cases}$$

When quantities are random, though, "distance" is harder to pin down — a difference could be small on one draw and large on the next. The rest of this section builds a concept of stochastic distance that survives that randomness.

Expectation and variance of the sample mean

The question: how far is a random variable from its own mean, as the sample size grows? Two facts, already established, do all the work:

$$\mathbb{E}[\bar X_n] = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right] = \mu, \qquad \mathbb{V}[\bar X_n] = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\left(\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n X_i - \mathbb{E}[\bar X_n]\right)^2\right] = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$

The question of the day

What is the probability that the sample mean \(\bar X_n\) is more than \(d\) away from its own mean \(\mu\)? In symbols, \(p[|\bar X_n - \mu| \gt d]\). The plan is to bound that probability, and the key trick — a little like a magic trick — is that this event is identical to \(p[(\bar X_n - \mu)^2 \gt d^2]\), since \(|w| = \sqrt{w^2}\) for any \(w\): squaring both sides of an absolute-value inequality preserves it exactly.

Chebyshev's inequality and the WLLN

Markov's inequality says that for any non-negative random variable \(W\) and any \(d^2 \gt 0\), \(p[W \gt d^2] \le \mathbb{E}[W]/d^2\). Apply it to \(W = (\bar X_n-\mu)^2\), whose expectation is exactly \(\mathbb{V}[\bar X_n] = \sigma^2/n\) from the last section:

$$p\big[(\bar X_n-\mu)^2 \gt d^2\big] \le \frac{\mathbb{E}\big[(\bar X_n-\mu)^2\big]}{d^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{nd^2}$$

and since \((\bar X_n-\mu)^2 \gt d^2\) is the same event as \(|\bar X_n - \mu| \gt d\), this is Chebyshev's inequality:

$$p\big[|\bar X_n - \mu| \gt d\big] \le \frac{\sigma^2}{nd^2}$$

For any fixed tolerance \(d\), the right-hand side goes to zero as \(n \to \infty\) — the probability of the sample mean straying far from \(\mu\) is squeezed to nothing. That is the weak law of large numbers: \(\bar X_n\) converges in probability to \(\mu\). Part 2 · Monte Carlo

Monte Carlo integration

The WLLN gives a deep, practical insight: if \(X_1, X_2, \dots\) is iid with finite mean and variance, the sample mean \(\bar X_n\) converges in probability to \(\mathbb{E}[X]\) — a fact you can use as a computational tool, not just a statistical one. Simulating a process and averaging to approximate the solution to a hard problem is Monte Carlo integration.

Monte Carlo for a region: computing π

Draw a large set of values \(x_i\) distributed \(F(x)\), and evaluate the indicator \(\mathbb{I}\{x_i \text{ is in } A\}\); average it, \(\bar p = \frac1n\sum_i \mathbb{I}\{x_i \in A\}\). The WLLN implies \(\bar p \to_{pr} p(A) = \int_{x \in A} f(x)\,dx\) — counting the fraction of random draws landing in a region estimates that region's probability (and, for a uniform distribution, its area).

The classic application: scatter points uniformly over the unit square and keep those with \(x^2+y^2 \lt 1\) — inside the quarter disc. The quarter disc has area \(\pi/4\) out of the square's area 1, so the kept fraction converges to \(\pi/4\):

$$\hat\pi_n = 4\cdot\frac{\#\{i : x_i^2+y_i^2 \lt 1\}}{n}$$

a \(\pi\)-estimator built from nothing but a random number generator and a comparison. The Class 09 lab's dart-throwing widget builds exactly this, live.

Monte Carlo for a function: computing an expectation

The same recipe generalizes beyond regions: draw \(x_i\) from \(F(x)\), evaluate any function \(g(x_i) = y_i\), and average: \(\bar y_n = \frac1n\sum_i y_i\). The WLLN implies \(\bar y_n \to_{pr} \int f(x)\,g(x)\,dx = \mathbb{E}[g(X)]\).

Suppose \(g(x) = \sin(x)\) and \(X\) is standard normal:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \sin(x)\,\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/2}\,dx$$

There is no obvious trig trick to compute that by hand — so simulate it instead: draw 10,000 standard normal variates \([X_1,\dots,X_n]\), compute \(g(X_i)\) for each, and estimate the integral as a plain average:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] \approx \hat E = \frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n g(x_i)$$

Sampling variation

\(\hat E\) is itself a sample mean, so the WLLN's own machinery applies to it directly — its variance is:

$$\mathbb{V}[\hat E] \approx \frac{s^2_{\hat E}}{n}$$

A tempting mistake

The right way to compute a Monte Carlo estimate is the plain average:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] \approx \frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n g(x_i)$$

and not

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] \neq \sum_{i=1}^n g(x_i)\,f(x_i)$$

for two reasons: the sample \([X_1,\dots,X_n]\) is already "weighted" by the distribution — the draws naturally cluster according to \(f(x)\), so forcing the density into the calculation again double-counts it — and the second form implicitly needs a grid spacing \(dx\) that was never sampled, turning Monte Carlo back into ordinary quadrature. The sample already comes from \(F\) and reflects the relative probabilities on its own; that is the entire trick.

The Class 09 lab states this same warning as a callout at exactly the point a reader would be tempted to make the mistake.

FAQ

Why does squaring both sides of \(|\bar X_n - \mu| \gt d\) help?

Because it turns an absolute-value inequality into one about a non-negative quantity, \((\bar X_n-\mu)^2\), which is exactly the form Markov's inequality needs — Markov's inequality only applies to non-negative random variables. Squaring is the bridge between "distance" and a quantity with a known expectation (the variance).

Is the weak law of large numbers a guarantee about any single run?

No — it is a guarantee about probability, not about certainty. A given sample mean might land far from \(\mu\) by chance; the WLLN only says that the probability of landing far away shrinks to zero as \(n\) grows, not that every individual sample mean is close.

Why does the "tempting mistake" formula fail specifically, rather than just being less accurate?

Because it changes what is being computed, not how precisely it is computed. \(\sum_i g(x_i)f(x_i)\) with no \(dx\) and no normalizing \(1/n\) is not an approximation to any integral at all — it conflates two entirely different estimation schemes (sampling-based Monte Carlo and grid-based quadrature) into a formula that is well-defined but answers neither question correctly.
