Bootstrap Inference & the Central Limit Theorem

Standardizing the bootstrap into Z-statistics, then replacing resampling with a closed-form bell curve
Understanding Uncertainty · lecture notes, digitized

Every bootstrap confidence interval and every bootstrap p-value is built from the same standardized quantity, a Z-statistic. This lecture's first half derives that machinery in full generality. Its second half asks why a bootstrap distribution built from resampling so often looks like a bell curve — the answer is the Central Limit Theorem — and shows that once you believe it, you can skip resampling entirely and read the same confidence intervals and p-values straight off the standard normal.

Part 1 · Z-Statistics & Bootstrap Inference

Standard errors

The standard deviation of a sampling distribution is its standard error — a large part of a statistician's job, professionally, is computing standard errors. For the sample mean:

$$\widehat{SE}(\bar X_n) = \sqrt{\mathbb{V}[\bar X_n]} = \frac{\hat s}{\sqrt n}$$

Z-statistics

Standardize each bootstrap replicate the same way any quantity gets standardized — center it, then divide by its own spread:

$$Z_b = \frac{\hat S_b - \hat S}{\widehat{SE}(\hat S)}$$

\(Z_b\) answers, literally, "how many standard-error units away from \(\hat S\) is my \(b\)-th bootstrap estimate?" Centering and normalizing this way doesn't change the rank of the replicates — the percentile confidence interval built from \([Z_1,\dots,Z_B]\) picks out the same replicates it always would — but it does make Z-values comparable across different statistics and different datasets, which raw \(\hat S_b\) values never are.

The percentile confidence interval, normalized

The Z-statistics \([Z_1,\dots,Z_B]\) approximate the distribution of the population quantity \(Z = (\hat S - S)/SE(\hat S)\). Find the \(q_{\alpha/2}\) and \(q_{1-\alpha/2}\) quantiles of the Z-sample and build a normalized confidence interval:

$$\left(\hat S - q_{\alpha/2}\times\widehat{SE}(\hat S),\ \ \hat S + q_{1-\alpha/2}\times\widehat{SE}(\hat S)\right)$$

The pattern "\(\hat S \pm q\times\widehat{SE}(\hat S)\)" is the foundation of confidence-interval construction generally — it reappears constantly for the rest of this course.

Hypothesis tests

Every confidence interval doubles as a statistical test. The null hypothesis of 0 is that the true value of \(S\) is zero; the alternative is that it isn't. Checking whether 0 falls inside the \(\alpha\)-level confidence interval settles the test: inside, fail to reject the null at level \(\alpha\); outside, reject it. Researchers are usually hoping to reject the null of zero — no one runs an expensive clinical trial hoping the treatment effect is exactly zero — and being upfront about that motivation is more honest than pretending the analysis has no stake in the outcome.

p-values

Picking a level \(\alpha\) in advance can feel arbitrary and ritualistic. The alternative question: "what confidence level \(\alpha\) would be just barely enough to reject the null of 0?" That threshold is the p-value — equivalently, "what fraction of the bootstrapped Z-values are farther from 0 than my observed Z-value?"

With a hypothesized value \(S_0\) (often 0), ask how often the distance from a bootstrap replicate to \(\hat S\) exceeds the distance from \(\hat S\) to \(S_0\):

$$p = \frac1B\sum_{b=1}^B \mathbb{I}\{|Z_b| \gt |Z_0|\} = \frac1B\sum_{b=1}^B \mathbb{I}\big\{|\hat S_b-\hat S| \gt |\hat S - S_0|\big\}$$

Bell curves: a first look at the CLT

The bell curve keeps showing up in this course. Why? Adding up many small deviations from zero and averaging them — under quite general conditions — produces a normal distribution. Standardize any normal distribution the same way \(Z\)-values are standardized, and you get a standard normal — a bell. The core observation of asymptotic statistics is that under the right circumstances, the ECDF of a standardized statistic can be approximated by the standard normal distribution. That observation is the Central Limit Theorem, and Part 2 is entirely about it.

Part 2 · The CLT and the Z/t-Tests

If the ECDF and quantiles from the bootstrap in Part 1 are replaced with the standard normal distribution and its inverse, the result is the classic z-test. That substitution is the entire content of this half of the lecture.

Asymptotic statistics

Everywhere Part 1 used the bootstrapped distribution of Z-statistics, this half substitutes the standard normal \(\Phi(z)\) — trading a resampling loop for a closed-form lookup, whenever that substitution is justified.

The Central Limit Theorem

Why is approximating the bootstrapped Z-distribution with \(\Phi(z)\) justified in the first place?

Central Limit Theorem

Let \(X_1,\dots,X_n\) be an iid sample of size \(n\) from a distribution with mean \(\mathbb{E}[X_i]=\mu\) and finite variance \(\mathbb{V}[X_i]=\sigma^2 \lt \infty\). Then as \(n \to \infty\), the distribution of \(Z_n = \sqrt n(\bar X_n-\mu)/\sigma\) converges to the standard normal, \(\Phi(z)\).

Using the CLT

Every bootstrap inference in Part 1 used a bootstrapped sampling distribution built by resampling. The CLT says that for large samples, the sampling distribution of a statistic is always approximately bell-shaped — so the standard normal can be substituted for the bootstrapped distribution, and confidence intervals and p-values become "asymptotically, approximately exact" closed-form calculations instead of simulations.

Confidence intervals via the CLT

Part 1 built confidence intervals from the \(q_{\alpha/2}\) and \(q_{1-\alpha/2}\) quantiles of the bootstrapped \([Z_1,\dots,Z_B]\):

$$\left(\hat S - q_{\alpha/2}\times\widehat{SE}(\hat S),\ \ \hat S + q_{1-\alpha/2}\times\widehat{SE}(\hat S)\right)$$

The CLT says those same quantiles can instead be read directly off the standard normal's own quantile function, with no resampling at all:

Confidence level\(\alpha\)\(\pm q\)
90%0.101.645
95%0.051.960
99%0.012.576

p-values via the CLT

Part 1's bootstrap p-value,

$$p = \frac1B\sum_{b=1}^B \mathbb{I}\{|Z_b| \gt |Z_0|\}$$

becomes, in the limit, an exact probability computed from the standard normal instead of an average over resamples:

$$p = p\!\left[\left|\frac{\hat S - S_0}{\widehat{SE}(\hat S)}\right| \gt |z|\right] = 2\bigl(1-\Phi(|z|)\bigr)$$

This is exactly the closed-form p-value the Class 11 lab builds as a live, draggable widget — no resampling loop, just a lookup against the standard normal CDF.

The t-distribution

The bootstrap derives a sampling distribution empirically from your data; the CLT assumes you have enough data that the sampling distribution is already normal. Between those two extremes: acknowledge the sample size honestly, but use a parametric distribution instead of either the non-parametric bootstrap or the large-sample normal. This is Student's t-distribution, with density

$$f(t) = \frac{\Gamma\!\left(\frac{d+1}{2}\right)}{\sqrt{\pi d}\,\Gamma\!\left(\frac d2\right)}\left(1+\frac{t^2}{d}\right)^{-\frac{d+1}{2}}$$

where \(d\) is the degrees of freedom and \(\Gamma(\cdot)\) is the gamma function. Around \(d \ge 32\), this density is already very close to the standard normal; for smaller \(d\) it puts more weight in the tails, giving a small-sample correction that keeps tests honestly calibrated. Rule of thumb: small sample, use a t-distribution; large sample, use the standard normal.

Two-sample t-tests

Observe two independent groups \(A\) and \(B\), with sample sizes \(n_A, n_B\), sample means \(\bar X_A, \bar X_B\), and sample variances \(s_A^2, s_B^2\). The question: is the difference in sample means large relative to the noise in both samples? The null hypothesis is usually \(H_0 : \mu_A - \mu_B = 0\). The standard error of the difference in means:

$$\widehat{SE}(\bar X_A - \bar X_B) = \sqrt{\frac{s_A^2}{n_A} + \frac{s_B^2}{n_B}}$$

and the two-sample t-statistic:

$$t = \frac{(\bar X_A-\bar X_B) - 0}{\sqrt{s_A^2/n_A + s_B^2/n_B}}$$

Compare this to the standard normal in large samples, or to a Student t-distribution in smaller ones. The full recipe: compute the difference in means; compute the pooled standard error; divide to get the t-statistic; compare it to the standard normal or t-distribution for a p-value, or build a confidence interval \((\bar X_A-\bar X_B) \pm q\times\widehat{SE}(\bar X_A-\bar X_B)\). This is the default test behind questions like "is average blood pressure different across two treatments," "is mean price different across two neighborhoods," or "is average product quality different across two factories."

Caution · paired data is not this test

If the same units are measured twice (before/after, matched pairs), this is not a two-independent-samples problem — it's a paired-difference problem, and needs a different standard error that accounts for the correlation between the two measurements on the same unit.

FAQ

Why does standardizing bootstrap replicates into Z-values not change the confidence interval's endpoints?

Because standardizing is a strictly increasing (monotonic) transformation — it preserves rank order. The 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the raw replicates map to exactly the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles of the standardized ones; only the number line they're read off of changes, not which replicates get selected.

Why does the 95% quantile table say 1.960 and not something rounder?

Because 1.960 is the exact (to three decimals) value of \(z\) such that \(\Phi(z) - \Phi(-z) = 0.95\) for the standard normal — it falls out of the normal CDF's inverse, not from any rounding convention. It's worth memorizing precisely because it appears so often.

When should I prefer the t-distribution over the standard normal?

Whenever your sample size is small (rule of thumb: well under 32) and you are estimating the standard error from the sample itself rather than knowing it exactly. The t-distribution's heavier tails correct for the extra uncertainty in that estimated standard error; as the sample grows, that correction shrinks and the t-distribution converges to the standard normal.
