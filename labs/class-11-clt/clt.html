The Central Limit Theorem

Why the average of skewed data is nonetheless a bell curve
Understanding Uncertainty · a build-it-from-scratch walkthrough

Used-car prices are badly lopsided: a wall of cheap beaters near $2k and a long tail of trucks past $30k. Nothing about that shape is a bell. Yet if you repeatedly grab 30 cars at random and write down their average price, those averages pile up into a clean, symmetric bell — centered on the true mean, and tighter every time you enlarge the sample. That is the central limit theorem, and this lab builds it from a single loop.

Built on the Class 11 notebooks 10_1_bootstrap_inference and 10_2_CLT. Data: 92 used-car listings scraped from Charlottesville Craigslist (cville_cars.csv), prices in thousands of dollars.

Start with the population itself, because the whole point of the CLT is that its shape does not matter. Our 92 prices have a mean of about \(\$8.6\text{k}\) and a standard deviation of about \(\$8.1\text{k}\) — a spread almost as large as the mean, which is the fingerprint of a long right tail.

Write the population mean \(\mu\) and variance \(\sigma^2\) plainly. These are fixed numbers describing the population, not estimates:

$$\mu = \mathbb{E}[X], \qquad \sigma^2 = \mathbb{V}[X] = \mathbb{E}\big[(X-\mu)^2\big]$$

The CLT needs only two things from this population: the observations are iid, and the variance \(\sigma^2\) is finite. Skew, multiple peaks, hard floors at zero — none of it disqualifies the population. Switch the simulator to the exponential or bimodal population to confirm the punchline survives shapes even uglier than the car prices.

The sample mean

  • Share · 2 minAgree on one sentence explaining why the average is steadier than a single draw, then read on to see it made exact.

The object the CLT is about is the sample mean — the average of one sample of size \(n\):

$$\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i$$

\(\bar{X}_n\) is itself random: draw a new sample of \(n\) cars and you get a slightly different average. Collecting many of those averages and histogramming them is exactly what the simulator's bottom plot does. Two facts about that histogram are true for any population with finite variance, no approximation involved — they follow directly from the linearity of expectation and the independence of the draws:

$$\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n] = \mu, \qquad \mathbb{V}[\bar{X}_n] = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$

The first says the averages center on the population mean — the red μ line runs through the middle of the bottom histogram at every \(n\). The second says their variance is the population variance divided by \(n\): more cars per sample, less scatter among the averages. The CLT adds one more claim on top of these two exact facts — that the shape of the histogram becomes Normal:

$$\bar{X}_n \;\approx\; \text{Normal}\!\left(\mu,\; \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \quad \text{for large } n$$
The claim, in one line

Center, spread, and shape are three separate promises. Centering (\(\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n]=\mu\)) and spread (\(\mathbb{V}[\bar{X}_n]=\sigma^2/n\)) hold at every \(n\), exactly. The CLT is the third promise: the shape turns into a bell as \(n\) grows, whatever the population looked like.

The √n law

  • Share · 2 minCommit to the factor, then find the rows in the table below where \(n\) quadruples and check the standard error halves.

Taking the square root of the variance \(\sigma^2/n\) gives the standard deviation of the sample mean, which has its own name — the standard error:

$$\operatorname{SE}(\bar{X}_n) = \sqrt{\mathbb{V}[\bar{X}_n]} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$

The \(\sqrt{n}\) in the denominator is the whole economics of sampling. Precision improves with more data, but only as fast as \(\sqrt{n}\): to halve the standard error you must quadruple the sample. The table below runs the simulator across a ladder of sample sizes and puts the theoretical \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\) next to the observed spread of the sample means.

σ/√n, predicted vs. observed

Read down the "theory" column: quadrupling \(n\) from 5 to 20-ish, or 25 to 100, cuts the standard error roughly in half. The observed column tracks it row for row. This law is exact for the spread at every \(n\) — the approximation in the CLT is only ever about the shape, never the center or the spread.

Standardizing

Think · Pair · Share · one curve to rule them all

To compare across populations and sample sizes, standardize the sample mean: subtract its center \(\mu\) and divide by its standard error \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\). This strips out the particular units and spread, leaving a pure shape:

$$Z_n = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \;\xrightarrow[n\to\infty]{}\; \text{Normal}(0, 1)$$

This is the form stated in the notebook, where \(T_n = \sqrt{n}\,(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/\sigma\) is the same quantity rearranged. Standardizing collapses every case onto one fixed target — the standard normal \(N(0,1)\) — so the blue curve below never moves. Only the histogram changes as you switch populations and slide \(n\).

Standardized sample mean · collapse onto N(0,1)
Histogram of \(Z = (\bar{X}-\mu)/(\sigma/\sqrt{n})\) against the fixed standard normal. At \(n=1\) the histogram still carries the population's skew; grow \(n\) and every population snaps onto the same bell.
Note · how large is "large enough"?

There is no universal \(n\). A nearly-symmetric population is Normal-looking by \(n \approx 10\); the strongly skewed exponential and the car prices need more, often \(n \gtrsim 30\)-\(50\). The rule of thumb "\(n \ge 30\)" is a rough default, not a theorem — the heavier the skew, the larger the \(n\) the bell demands. Use the widget to see each population's own crossover.

Testing a hypothesized mean

Think · Pair · Share · closed form instead of resampling
  • Think · 2 minThe bootstrap lab tested a hypothesized value by checking whether it fell inside a resampled interval — thousands of resamples, then a lookup. Now that \(Z_n\) collapses onto the fixed \(N(0,1)\) curve, what could you compute directly, with no resampling at all, to measure how extreme an observed \(Z\) is?
I've done the Think step — reveal Pair & Share
  • Pair · 3 minCompare answers. Did you land on "the area under the standard normal curve beyond \(|Z|\)"? That area is a p-value, and it comes from a known formula, not a simulation.
  • Share · 2 minAgree on what a *small* area beyond \(|Z|\) should mean about the hypothesized value, then test your answer in the widget below.

A sample of size \(n\) gives you a mean \(\bar{X}_n\) and a standard error \(\widehat{SE} = \hat\sigma/\sqrt{n}\). To test a hypothesized population mean \(\mu_0\) — a null \(H_0: \mu = \mu_0\) against \(H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0\) — standardize the gap between what you observed and what you hypothesized:

$$Z = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu_0}{\widehat{SE}}$$

Under \(H_0\), the CLT says \(Z\) is (approximately) a draw from \(N(0,1)\). The p-value is the probability that a standard normal variable would land at least as far from zero as your observed \(Z\) — the two shaded tails below. Because \(\Phi\), the standard normal CDF, is a known closed-form function, this is a lookup, not a resampling loop:

$$p = 2\bigl(1 - \Phi(|Z|)\bigr)$$

A small p-value says the data would be surprising if \(H_0\) were true — the usual cutoff is \(p < 0.05\), which lines up exactly with \(\mu_0\) falling outside the 95% confidence interval from a moment ago. Drag \(\mu_0\) below and watch the shaded tail area track the verdict.

Test a hypothesized mean against a real sample
One fixed sample of \(n = 30\) car prices gives \(\bar{X}_n\) and \(\widehat{SE}\). The curve is the standard normal; the shaded tails mark everywhere at least as extreme as your observed \(Z\). Their combined area is the p-value.
What a p-value does and does not say

A p-value of 0.03 means: if \(H_0\) were exactly true, a sample this extreme or more so would turn up about 3% of the time. It is not "there is a 3% chance \(H_0\) is true," and it is not "there is a 97% chance \(H_1\) is true" — the null hypothesis either is or is not true, full stop, and the p-value only describes how surprising your data would be under it. Small p, surprising data under \(H_0\); that is all it claims.

Check your understanding — comparing two groups

The car-price z-test above compares one sample mean to a fixed \(\mu_0\). An A/B test asks the same question about two sample means at once: are group A and group B plausibly drawn from populations with the same mean? Suppose 40 manual-transmission listings average \(\bar{X}_A = 7.10\)\,$k with \(s_A = 5.20\), and 52 automatic listings average \(\bar{X}_B = 9.60\)\,$k with \(s_B = 6.80\). Compute a Z-statistic for \(H_0: \mu_A = \mu_B\) using the pooled standard error \(\sqrt{s_A^2/n_A + s_B^2/n_B}\), then decide whether to reject at the 5% level (\(|Z| > 1.96\)).

Show answer

The pooled SE is \(\sqrt{5.20^2/40 + 6.80^2/52} = \sqrt{0.676 + 0.889} = \sqrt{1.565} \approx 1.251\). The Z-statistic is \(Z = (7.10 - 9.60)/1.251 \approx -2.00\). Since \(|{-2.00}| > 1.96\), reject \(H_0\) at the 5% level: the two transmission types' average prices are not consistent with a single common mean. Same recipe as the one-sample test — subtract, divide by a standard error, compare to \(N(0,1)\) — applied to a difference of two means instead of one.

FAQ

Does the population have to be Normal for the CLT to work?

No — that is the entire point. The population can be skewed, bounded, or multi-peaked. The only requirements are that the draws are iid and the population variance is finite. The sample mean becomes Normal even when the raw data never does.

Why divide the spread by √n and not by n?

Because variances add for independent draws, and averaging divides by \(n\). The variance of the mean is \(\sigma^2/n\); taking the square root to get a standard deviation turns that into \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\). Precision therefore improves like \(\sqrt{n}\): quadruple the data to halve the standard error.

Is n = 30 a magic threshold?

No. It is a rough rule of thumb. Mildly non-normal populations are Normal-looking well before 30; heavily skewed ones can need far more. The standardized-view widget lets you watch each population reach the bell at its own pace.

My mean_of_means and sd_of_means are a little off from μ and σ/√n. Bug?

Probably not. With finite \(m\) these are Monte-Carlo estimates and jitter around the truth; larger \(m\) tightens them. The autograder uses a generous \(m\) and a tolerance for exactly this reason. If they are wildly off, check that you are averaging \(n\) draws (not summing) and dividing the variance by \(n\).
