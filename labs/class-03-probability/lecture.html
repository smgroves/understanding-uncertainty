Probability & Random Variables

From sets and outcomes to the machinery of expectation and variance

A generating process runs out in the world; sampling it produces data; fitting models to that data is how you learn about the process. All three steps are inherently probabilistic, which is why this lecture starts from raw sets and outcomes and builds up, in order, to the two numbers — expectation and variance — that summarize any random variable at all.

Part 1 · Probability

Sample spaces, events, and probability spaces

Roll two six-sided dice and there are 36 possible outcomes, laid out as an ordered pair for each die. Which outcomes make the sum even? Strictly less than 7? Make the two dice match? What proportion of the 36 satisfy each?

A set is just a collection of things. For two sets \(E\) and \(F\): the union \(E \cup F\) is everything in either one; the intersection \(E \cap F\) is everything in both; the complement of \(E\) in \(F\), written \(F \backslash E\) or \(E^c\), is everything in \(F\) but not \(E\). The empty set \(\varnothing\) has no elements at all.

A probability space \((\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{E}, p)\) has three parts: a sample space \(\mathcal{S}\) of possible outcomes, a set \(\mathcal{E}\) of events (subsets of \(\mathcal{S}\)), and a probability function \(p\) mapping events into \([0,1]\). Events combine outcomes — "roll an even number," "the roll sums to seven" — and the set of events \(\mathcal{E}\) always obeys four rules: nothing happening (\(\varnothing\)) has probability 0; something happening (\(\mathcal{S}\)) has probability 1; every event's complement is also an event; and the union and intersection of any two events are themselves events.

Additivity

Union becomes sum, as long as you don't double-count:

$$p(E \cup F) = p(E) + p(F) - \underbrace{p(E \cap F)}_{\text{correction for double-counting}}$$

If \(E \cap F = \varnothing\) (they share no outcomes), the correction term vanishes and \(p(E \cup F) = p(E) + p(F)\) exactly. In general \(p(E \cup F) \le p(E) + p(F)\), so probability is sub-additive.

Iterating this rule over disjoint events gives finite additivity: if \(E_1, \dots, E_n\) are mutually disjoint (no two share an outcome),

$$p\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n E_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^n p(E_i)$$

Complementary events

The probability of an event not happening is 1 minus the probability it happens. Since \(E\) and its complement \(E^c\) are disjoint by definition and together cover everything, \(\mathcal{S} = E \cup E^c\):

$$p(\mathcal{S}) = p(E \cup E^c) = p(E) + p(E^c) = 1 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad p(E^c) = 1 - p(E)$$

Conditional probability, independence, and Bayes' rule

Information is about renormalizing to account for a shrinkage of the possibilities. The conditional probability of \(A\) given \(B\):

$$p[A\mid B] = \frac{p[A\cap B]}{p[B]}$$

Sum the probability that \(A\) and \(B\) occur together, then renormalize by \(p[B]\) so the result properly accounts for already knowing \(B\) happened. Event \(A\) is independent of \(B\) exactly when \(p[A \cap B] = p[A]\,p[B]\) — their joint probability is not affected by whether the other one occurred.

Knowing \(B\) happened also lets you renormalize an expectation:

$$\mathbb{E}[X\mid B] = \sum_x x \times p[X=x\mid B] = \sum_x x \times \frac{p[X=x\cap B]}{p[B]}$$

Flipping the roles of \(A\) and \(B\) in the conditional-probability definition gives \(p[B\mid A]\,p[A] = p[A\cap B]\); substituting that into the numerator of \(p[A\mid B]\) gives Bayes' rule:

$$p[A\mid B] = \frac{p[B\mid A]\times p[A]}{p[B]}$$

Bayes' rule is usually read as \(\underbrace{p[\text{hypothesis}\mid\text{data}]}_{\text{posterior}} = \dfrac{\underbrace{p[\text{data}\mid\text{hypothesis}]}_{\text{likelihood}} \times \underbrace{p[\text{hypothesis}]}_{\text{prior}}}{\underbrace{p[\text{data}]}_{\text{marginal}}}\) — a foundational piece of probabilistic reasoning this course returns to repeatedly.

Part 2 · Random Variables

A coin flip as a gamble

Flip a coin: heads wins a dollar, tails loses one.

$$X(s) = \begin{cases} +1, & s = H \\ -1, & s = T \end{cases}$$

This is a gamble — an assignment of numbers to real-world outcomes. If the coin is fair, you expect \(\tfrac12(+1) + \tfrac12(-1) = 0\). Would you take that gamble? What about \(\pm 10\), \(\pm 100\), \(\pm 1000\) — does your willingness change even though the expected value is 0 every time?

A random variable is a function \(X\) assigning numbers to outcomes: \(X : \mathcal{S} \to \mathbb{R}\). Think of it as a bet — on a die, the stock market, any game — and the random variable is the money that changes hands depending on what happens. The probability space \((\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{E}, p)\) represents fundamental uncertainty about the world itself — an outcome could be a temperature, an ice cream flavor, anything — and the random variable is what assigns a number to each one: \(X(95°F) = -6\), \(X(\text{vanilla}) = 4.25\). Choosing sensible numbers is where domain expertise matters.

The probability mass function

Once outcomes are mapped to numbers, ask: what is the probability \(X\) takes a given value? For each value \(x\), the probability mass function gives \(p[X=x]\) — the probability of every outcome \(s\) with \(X(s) = x\). If \(X\) only takes values \(x_1, \dots, x_L\), those probabilities sum to 1: \(\sum_{\ell=1}^L p[X=x_\ell] = 1\).

The random variable is what lets you stop tracking every conceivable event and track only the ones that matter for \(X\) — it partitions the state space by the values \(X\) takes, and "pushes" the whole probability space forward onto just those numbers. Betting on whether a die roll is even or odd rather than which face comes up:

$$X(s) = \begin{cases} +1, & s \text{ is odd, you win} \\ -1, & s \text{ is even, you lose} \end{cases}$$

needs only four events — \(\varnothing\), \(\{1,3,5\}\), \(\{2,4,6\}\), and \(\{1,\dots,6\}\), with probabilities 0, 1/2, 1/2, and 1 — instead of tracking all 6 individual faces separately.

Expectation

Suppose \(X\) takes only values \(x_1, \dots, x_L\), each with probability \(p_1, \dots, p_L\). A pessimist expects the minimum, an optimist the maximum; something in between is the expectation:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = p_1x_1 + p_2x_2 + \cdots + p_Lx_L = \sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell x_\ell$$

Weight each outcome by how often it occurs, and sum. Expectation is linear: transforming every value by \(a + bX\),

$$\begin{alignat*}{2}
\sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell(a+bx_\ell) &=& \sum_{\ell=1}^L (p_\ell a + bp_\ell x_\ell) \quad &(\text{Definition})\\
&=& \sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell a + \sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell b x_\ell \quad &(\text{Break up the sum})\\
&=& a\sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell + b\sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell x_\ell \quad &(\text{Factor out } a \text{ and } b)\\
\underbrace{\sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell(a+bx_\ell)}_{\mathbb{E}[a+bX]} &=& a + b\underbrace{\sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell x_\ell}_{\mathbb{E}[X]} \quad &(\textstyle\sum p_\ell = 1)
\end{alignat*}$$

so \(\mathbb{E}[a+bX] = a + b\,\mathbb{E}[X]\) — expectation commutes with any linear function. Since \(\mathbb{E}[X]\) is itself just a number, \(\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X]] = \mathbb{E}[X]\). It only commutes with linear functions in general (Jensen's inequality is the statement of how it fails for non-linear ones).

Variance

The variance of \(X\) is the expected squared deviation from its own expectation — how dispersed \(X\) is around its center:

$$\mathbb{V}[X] = \mathbb{E}\big[(X-\mathbb{E}[X])^2\big] = \sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell(x_\ell - \mathbb{E}[X])^2$$

Expanding that square gives the computational shortcut used constantly throughout this course:

$$\begin{alignat*}{2}
\mathbb{V}[X] &=& \mathbb{E}\big[(X-\mathbb{E}[X])^2\big] \quad &(\text{Definition})\\
&=& \mathbb{E}\big[X^2 - 2X\mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[X]^2\big] \quad &(\text{FOIL})\\
&=& \mathbb{E}[X^2] - 2\mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[X]^2 \quad &(\text{Linearity})\\
&=& \mathbb{E}[X^2] - \mathbb{E}[X]^2 \quad &(\text{Simplify})
\end{alignat*}$$

Variance does not commute with a linear transformation the same simple way expectation does. For \(Y = a + bX\):

$$\begin{alignat*}{2}
\mathbb{V}[Y] &=& \sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell(y_\ell - \mathbb{E}[Y])^2 \quad &(\text{Definition})\\
&=& \sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell(a+bx_\ell - \mathbb{E}[a+bX])^2 \quad &(Y=a+bX)\\
&=& \sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell(a+bx_\ell - a - b\mathbb{E}[X])^2 \quad &(\text{Linearity of } \mathbb{E})\\
&=& \sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell(bx_\ell - b\mathbb{E}[X])^2 \quad &(\text{Cancel the } a\text{'s})\\
&=& b^2\sum_{\ell=1}^L p_\ell(x_\ell-\mathbb{E}[X])^2 \quad &(\text{Factor out } b)\\
\mathbb{V}[Y] &=& b^2\,\mathbb{V}[X]
\end{alignat*}$$

Adding a constant \(a\) never changes the spread; scaling by \(b\) scales the variance by \(b^2\), not by \(b\) — variance is not \(a + b\,\mathbb{V}[X]\). The Class 03 lab specializes this whole machinery to one concrete random variable, the binomial, and builds its PMF from scratch.

FAQ

Why does variance scale by \(b^2\) and not by \(b\)?

Because variance is defined in squared units of \(X\) — it measures a squared deviation. Scaling \(X\) by \(b\) scales every deviation by \(b\), and squaring that deviation scales the variance by \(b^2\). Adding a constant \(a\) shifts every value equally without changing how spread out they are relative to each other, so it drops out entirely.

Is independence the same as "not correlated"?

Independence (\(p[A\cap B] = p[A]p[B]\)) is a stronger, more general condition than zero correlation. Two variables can have zero linear correlation while still being dependent in some non-linear way; genuine independence rules out any relationship, linear or not.

Why does Bayes' rule matter if it's "just" conditional probability rearranged?

Because the rearrangement flips which conditional probability you need. Often \(p[\text{data}\mid\text{hypothesis}]\) (the likelihood) is easy to compute or reason about directly, while \(p[\text{hypothesis}\mid\text{data}]\) (the posterior — what you actually want to know) is not. Bayes' rule is the bridge between the direction you can compute and the direction you care about.
