Why the same formula works everywhere

A sample proportion is the average of a 0/1 indicator over \(n\) observations:

$$\hat{p} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{I}\{c_i = \ell\}$$

Because each indicator is itself a Bernoulli trial with success probability \(p_\ell\), linearity of expectation makes \(\hat{p}_\ell\) unbiased, and treating "\(C = \ell\)" as its own coin flip gives the variance for free:

$$\mathbb{E}[\hat{p}_\ell] = p_\ell, \qquad \mathbb{V}[\hat{p}_\ell] = \frac{p_\ell(1-p_\ell)}{n}$$

The standard error — the square root of that variance — is what the shaded band in the Try It widget draws around \(\hat{p}\). Nothing here is specific to survival, triage, or any other label: any proportion computed from an iid sample has exactly this variance. The full derivation, starting from a single coin flip, is in the lecture.

The ECDF is a proportion too

  • Share · 2 minAgree on a predicted threshold where the error band should be widest, then drag the slider below to check.

Fix a heart-rate threshold \(x\). The indicator \(\mathbb{I}\{\text{HR}_i \le x\}\) is 1 for every patient at or below \(x\) and 0 otherwise — a Bernoulli trial with success probability \(F(x)\), the true fraction of the population below \(x\). Averaging it over the sample is the ECDF itself, so every property from the last two sections carries over unchanged.

ECDF of heart rate, with its own standard-error band
Drag the threshold. The dashed line is \(\hat{F}(x)\); the shaded band is \(\hat{F}(x) \pm \sqrt{\hat{F}(x)(1-\hat{F}(x))/n}\) — widest near the middle of the curve, narrowest in the tails.
Check your understanding

At HR = 60, \(\hat{F}(60) = 0.14\); at HR = 90, \(\hat{F}(90) = 0.83\). Without computing anything, which threshold has the larger standard error, and why?

Show answer

Neither is that close to 0.5, but 0.14 is farther from it than 0.83 is close to... checking precisely: \(p(1-p)\) at 0.14 is \(0.14 \times 0.86 = 0.1204\); at 0.83 it is \(0.83\times 0.17 = 0.1411\). The threshold at HR = 90 has the larger standard error, because 0.83 sits closer to 0.5 than 0.14 does. The rule is about distance from 0.5, not which side of it you are on.

FAQ

Is the ECDF's standard error the same at every threshold?

No — it depends on \(\hat{F}(x)\) itself through \(\hat{F}(x)(1-\hat{F}(x))\), which peaks at \(\hat{F}(x) = 0.5\) and shrinks toward the tails. The ECDF is least certain near its own median and most certain where almost all, or almost none, of the data has accumulated.

Why does one-hot encoding always sum to 1 across a row?

Because each patient has exactly one triage score — the indicator for their actual label is 1, and every other label's indicator is 0 for that same row. That is also why one column is always redundant given the rest, and why models with an intercept typically drop one dummy column.

Does a 91% survival proportion mean 91% of any new patient's chance to survive?

Only if this sample is representative of the population you care about predicting for. The proportion and its standard error describe how precisely this particular sample estimates the population probability \(p\) — they say nothing about whether this sample's population matches whatever new patient you are asking about.
