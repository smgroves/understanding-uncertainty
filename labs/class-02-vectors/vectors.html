Vectors, Distance & the Inner Product

One operation — multiply and add — underneath length, distance, covariance, and matrix multiplication
Understanding Uncertainty · a build-it-from-scratch walkthrough

Every patient in this lab's dataset is a point in space before it is anything else — a sodium reading and a chloride reading, two numbers, one dot on a plane. The single operation that measures how those points relate to each other — how far apart, how alike, how unrelated — is the inner product: multiply matching entries, add them up. Length, distance, covariance, and matrix multiplication are not four separate ideas to memorize. They are one idea, applied four times.

Built on the Class 02 notebooks 02_1_vectors_and_matrices and 02_2_ortho. Data: 28 patients' sodium and chloride readings (CardiacPatientData.csv, uu_fa26/class_02/).

Patients as points

A vector is a list of \(n\) numbers you can do arithmetic on. Write a row vector across and a column vector down:

$$x = [x_1, x_2, \dots, x_m], \qquad y = \left[\begin{array}{c} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ \vdots \\ y_n \end{array}\right]$$

In a dataframe, one observation — one patient's row — is a vector: its entries are that patient's Na, Cl, HR, and every other recorded variable. Plot two patients by just two of their coordinates, Na and Cl, and each patient becomes a literal point on a plane. Add a third coordinate, HR, and each patient becomes a point in three-dimensional space — the picture keeps making sense, only the space gets bigger.

28 patients as points · Na vs. Cl, colored by HR
Each dot is one patient's (Na, Cl) pair. Color encodes heart rate — the third coordinate you cannot see directly on a 2-D plot, but can still read off as a color.

NumPy is what turns this notation into code. x = np.array([139, 93]) builds the vector; x.shape reports its length. A stray dimension mismatch — a row where a column was expected — is the single most common bug in this material, and x.reshape(1,-1) (row) versus x.reshape(-1,1) (column) is the fix.

The inner product

Before computing on paper — what do you predict the sign of that sum will be, and what might a sum of exactly zero mean about how these two vectors point?
I've done the Think step — reveal Pair & Share
  • Pair · 3 minCompare: \(3(4) + 4(-3) = 12 - 12 = 0\). A sum of exactly zero is not a coincidence — it means the two vectors point in directions that share nothing, a relationship this section names next.
  • Share · 2 minAgree on what a large positive sum versus a large negative sum should mean, then read the definition below.

The inner product (or dot product) of two \(n\)-vectors multiplies matching entries and adds the results into a single number:

$$x \cdot y = x_1 y_1 + x_2 y_2 + \cdots + x_n y_n = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i y_i$$

In NumPy this is np.inner(x, y) or, more commonly, x @ y. On the toy pair \(x = (3,4)\), \(y = (4,-3)\): \(x \cdot y = 12 - 12 = 0\). Every other quantity on this page — length, distance, covariance, correlation, even matrix multiplication itself — is this one formula, reused.

Length and distance

Think · Pair · Share · dotting a vector with itself
  • Think · 2 minTake the inner product of \(x = (3, 4)\) with itself: \(3 \times 3 + 4 \times 4\). What ordinary geometric quantity do you get if you then take the square root? (Hint: 3, 4, and a famous third number form a well-known triangle.)
I've done the Think step — reveal Pair & Share
  • Pair · 3 minCompare: \(x \cdot x = 9 + 16 = 25\), and \(\sqrt{25} = 5\) — the length of the vector, and the hypotenuse of the 3-4-5 right triangle. The inner product of a vector with itself is the Pythagorean theorem in disguise.
  • Share · 2 minAgree on what \((x - y) \cdot (x - y)\) should measure, then check the formula below.

Dotting a vector with itself gives its squared length, so the length itself is the square root:

$$\|x\| = \sqrt{x \cdot x} = \sqrt{x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \cdots + x_n^2}$$

Length turns into distance the same way ordinary subtraction turns into a gap: the distance between two vectors is the length of their difference.

$$\|x - y\| = \sqrt{(x-y)\cdot(x-y)} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - y_i)^2}$$

On the toy pair, \(\|x\| = \|y\| = 5\) and \(\|x - y\| = \|(-1, 7)\| = \sqrt{1 + 49} = \sqrt{50} \approx 7.07\). This is exactly the formula behind every cell of the distance matrix in the Try It widget — patient by patient, \((\text{Na}_i - \text{Na}_j)^2 + (\text{Cl}_i - \text{Cl}_j)^2\), square-rooted.

Covariance: the statistical dot product

Think · Pair · Share · centering before dotting
  • Think · 2 minSodium and chloride are both electrolytes the kidneys regulate together. Before computing anything: do you expect patients with above-average Na to also tend to have above-average Cl, below-average, or no particular pattern? What sign would that prediction give an inner product of the two centered variables?
I've done the Think step — reveal Pair & Share
  • Pair · 3 minCompare predictions. Two variables that move together (both above average, or both below average, on the same patients) give a positive inner product once centered.
  • Share · 2 minAgree on a prediction for the sign, then check it against this sample's real covariance below.

Subtract each variable's own mean before dotting, and the inner product becomes a statistic. Let \(c_X = X - m(X)\) and \(c_Y = Y - m(Y)\) be the centered versions of two variables measured on the same \(N\) patients. Their covariance is a centered, rescaled dot product:

$$\mathrm{cov}(X,Y) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N (x_i - m(X))(y_i - m(Y)) = \frac{c_X \cdot c_Y}{N}$$

On this sample of 28 patients, \(\mathrm{cov}(\text{Na}, \text{Cl}) \approx 4.31\) — positive, confirming that patients with above-average sodium tend to also run above-average chloride. A covariance of zero would mean the two centered variables are orthogonal: knowing one gives you no linear information about the other.

Centering the patients · Na vs. Cl
Left: the raw scatter, off-center. Right: the same 28 patients after subtracting each variable's mean — the cloud recenters on the origin, and the inner product of the two centered coordinate lists is N times the covariance.

Orthogonality

Two vectors are orthogonal exactly when their inner product is zero: \(x \cdot y = 0\). The toy pair \((3,4)\) and \((4,-3)\) is orthogonal — that is the "share nothing" relationship the first Think prompt on this page was pointing at. Orthogonality is not a curiosity: it is the formal statement of independence between random variables, the optimality condition that pins down a regression's coefficients, and the organizing idea behind PCA. Every time you meet one of those later in the course, it is this same equation, \(x \cdot y = 0\), wearing a different hat.

Check your understanding

Is the vector \((1, 2, 3)\) orthogonal to \((3, 0, -1)\)? Compute the inner product to decide.

Show answer

\((1)(3) + (2)(0) + (3)(-1) = 3 + 0 - 3 = 0\). Yes — orthogonal, even though neither vector looks like it was constructed to cancel the other. The inner product is the only reliable way to check; nothing about the raw numbers gives it away by inspection.

Matrix multiplication, stacked

Think · Pair · Share · rows times columns
  • Think · 2 minMatrix multiplication has a famous, easy-to-misstate rule: to multiply \(A\) times \(B\), the number of columns in \(A\) must equal the number of rows in \(B\). Given everything on this page so far, what operation is being repeated over and over inside a matrix multiplication, and why would that operation force the two dimensions to match?
I've done the Think step — reveal Pair & Share
  • Pair · 3 minCompare answers. Matrix multiplication is a grid of inner products, one per (row, column) pair — and an inner product needs its two vectors to have matching length, which is exactly the shared dimension.
  • Share · 2 minAgree on what shape the result has, then check it against the formula below.

Multiplying a matrix \(A\) (\(n\) rows, \(m\) columns) by a column vector \(x\) (length \(m\)) computes the inner product of every row of \(A\) with \(x\):

$$Ax = \left[\begin{array}{c} a_{1,:} \cdot x \\ a_{2,:} \cdot x \\ \vdots \\ a_{n,:} \cdot x \end{array}\right]$$

Matrix-times-matrix is the same idea with the second argument's columns playing the role of \(x\), one at a time: entry \((r, c)\) of \(AB\) is row \(r\) of \(A\) dotted with column \(c\) of \(B\). That is why A @ B requires \(A\) to have as many columns as \(B\) has rows — every one of those inner products needs its two vectors to be the same length — and why it is not the same operation as A * B, which multiplies matching entries with no summation at all (the Hadamard product).

The distance matrix in the Try It widget is built the same "grid of paired calculations" way, but with NumPy's broadcasting standing in for an explicit double loop: reshape one list of patients to a row and the other to a column, subtract, and NumPy expands the mismatch into a full grid automatically — x.reshape(1,-1) - y.reshape(-1,1) — the same computation as writing two nested for loops, without writing them.

FAQ

Is the inner product the same thing as A * B in NumPy?

No — that is the single most common mix-up in this material. A * B multiplies matching entries with no summation (the Hadamard product) and requires A and B to be the same shape. The inner product sums those products into one number, and matrix multiplication (A @ B) is a whole grid of inner products, which is why it needs the "columns of A equal rows of B" rule instead of matching shapes.

Why does covariance divide by N instead of using the raw sum?

Dividing by \(N\) makes covariance an average paired deviation, so it does not simply grow because you added more patients to the sample — the same way a mean does not grow just because \(n\) grew. The raw sum \(c_X \cdot c_Y\) does depend on \(N\); the covariance is designed not to.

Does zero covariance mean the two variables are unrelated?

Only in the linear sense. Covariance and its normalized cousin, correlation, only detect straight-line relationships. Two variables can be strongly related in a curved, non-linear way and still have covariance at or near zero — orthogonality here is a statement about linear structure, not about dependence in general.

Why does matrix multiplication require matching inner dimensions?

Because every entry of the product is an inner product of a row from the first matrix with a column from the second, and an inner product is undefined unless its two vectors have the same length. \(A\) having \(m\) columns and \(B\) having \(m\) rows is exactly what makes every one of those row-column pairings a valid, equal-length dot product.
