Vectors, Matrices & the Inner Product

From dataframe notation to the one operation underneath length, distance, and matrix multiplication
Understanding Uncertainty · lecture notes, digitized

A dataframe is already a matrix wearing a costume: rows are observations, columns are variables, and NumPy is what lets you compute on all of them at once instead of writing loops. This lecture builds that notation up from a single vector, then spends its second half on the inner product — the single operation that turns out to be length, distance, covariance, and matrix multiplication, each in a different disguise.

Part 1 · Vectors and Matrices

Vectors: notation and real examples

A dataframe's variables are columns; its observations are rows. A vector is a list of \(n\) numbers you can do arithmetic on — the natural notation for either one. Write a row vector across, a column vector down:

$$x = [x_1, x_2, x_3, \dots, x_m], \qquad y = \left[\begin{array}{c} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ \vdots \\ y_n \end{array}\right]$$

Three real examples ground this immediately. Loading cville_cars.csv and plotting miles against price makes every listing a literal 2-vector, a point on a plane. Coloring that same scatter by age (with price on a log scale) turns each listing into a 3-vector — two plotted coordinates plus a third encoded as color. The same trick works on CardiacPatientData.csv: plotting Cl against Na, colored by HR, makes each patient a 3-vector the same way.

Transpose and indexing

The transpose flips a vector from row to column or column to row:

$$x^\top = \left[\begin{array}{c} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_m \end{array}\right], \qquad y^\top = [y_1, y_2, \dots, y_n]$$

As a data scientist, "row" always means observation and "column" always means variable — the transpose is a bookkeeping flip, not a change of meaning. Python (like most programming languages) indexes from 0 through \(m-1\), not 1 through \(m\): \(x = [x_0, x_1, \dots, x_{m-1}]\). This trips up anyone coming from R or MATLAB, which index from 1. Relatedly, range(n) enumerates \(0, 1, \dots, n-1\), not \(1, \dots, n\).

NumPy arrays and reshaping

Vanilla Python has no vector class; numpy supplies one. x = np.array(vals) builds a vector from any list of numbers, and x.shape reports its length.

x = np.array([-1, 2, 3, 0])
print(f'The vector x: \n {x}')
print(f"x's shape: {x.shape}")

NumPy internally treats every vector as one-dimensional, then sometimes demands an explicit reshape to a row or column: x.reshape(1, -1) or x[None, :] for a row; x.reshape(-1, 1) or x[:, None] for a column.

Mean and variance, loop vs. NumPy

The sample mean of a vector \(X\):

$$m(X) = \frac{x_1+x_2+\cdots+x_n}{n} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n x_i$$

Every summation \(\sum_{i=1}^n\) is a loop over range(n) in vanilla Python:

n = len(x)
sum = 0
for i in range(n):
    sum += x[i]
mean = sum / n

NumPy replaces that entire loop with x.mean(). The mean answers a thought experiment: if you drew one value from the sample at random, each with probability \(1/n\), what value would you expect? A related number, the sample variance, measures how far away values tend to be from that mean:

$$s^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \bar{x})^2$$

Again a loop in vanilla Python, and again one call — x.var() — in NumPy.

Vector addition and matrices

Two vectors of the same length add entrywise, and a scalar multiplies every entry:

$$x+y = \left[\begin{array}{c}x_1+y_1\\x_2+y_2\\\vdots\\x_n+y_n\end{array}\right], \qquad sx = \left[\begin{array}{c}sx_1\\sx_2\\\vdots\\sx_n\end{array}\right]$$

Both operations are associative and commutative, since ordinary addition and multiplication are. A matrix is just a dataframe by another name: stack \(n\) row vectors of length \(m\) and you get an \(m\)-row-by-\(n\)-column matrix (think: combining observations into a dataframe); stack \(m\) column vectors of length \(n\) side by side and you get the same shape read the other way (think: combining variables into a dataframe).

Hadamard product and broadcasting

Adding two matrices of the same shape adds entry by entry, same as vectors. Multiplying two matrices entry by entry — A * B in NumPy — is the Hadamard product, and it is explicitly not matrix multiplication, a distinction Part 2 makes precise.

NumPy's broadcasting rule lets you subtract a row vector from a column vector of a different length and get a full grid of pairwise results, with no explicit double loop:

$$[x_1,\dots,x_n] - \left[\begin{array}{c}y_1\\\vdots\\y_m\end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{ccc}x_1-y_1 & \cdots & x_n-y_1\\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ x_1-y_m & \cdots & x_n-y_m\end{array}\right]$$
y = np.array([2, -3, 11])
print(f'x: {x.reshape(1, -1)}')
print(f'y: {y.reshape(-1, 1)}')
print('\n Subtraction:')
print(x.reshape(1, -1) - y.reshape(-1, 1))
print('\n Comparison:')
print(x.reshape(1, -1) <= y.reshape(-1, 1))
print('\n Distance:')
print(np.sqrt((x.reshape(1, -1) - y.reshape(-1, 1)) ** 2))

That last line — reshape one list to a row, another to a column, subtract, square, square-root — is a full pairwise distance computation with no visible loop. The Class 02 lab builds exactly this as a live 28-patient distance matrix you can hover over.

Part 2 · The Inner Product

The inner product

The inner product (or dot product) of two \(n\)-vectors is the most fundamental calculation in this course, and arguably in machine learning generally — multiply matching entries, add the results:

$$x\cdot y = x_1y_1+x_2y_2+\cdots+x_ny_n = \sum_{i=1}^n x_iy_i$$

In NumPy, np.inner(x, y) or, far more commonly, x @ y. The inner product is what creates the geometric shape of a vector space — length, angle, and distance all fall out of it, in the sections that follow.

Length and distance

Dotting a vector with itself gives its squared length:

$$\|x\| = \sqrt{x\cdot x} = \sqrt{x_1^2+x_2^2+\cdots+x_n^2}$$

— np.sqrt(np.inner(x, x)), or equivalently np.sqrt(x @ x). This is the ordinary absolute value \(|x| = \sqrt{x^2}\), generalized from one number to \(n\) of them. Distance follows immediately: the length of the difference between two vectors,

$$\|x-y\| = \sqrt{(x-y)\cdot(x-y)} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^N (x_i-y_i)^2}$$

— np.sqrt(((x - y) ** 2).sum()) — generalizing the familiar \(|x-y| = \sqrt{(x-y)^2}\).

Covariance: the statistical dot product

Center two variables \(X\) and \(Y\) measured on the same \(N\) observations — subtract each one's own mean, \(c_X = X - m(X)\), \(c_Y = Y - m(Y)\) — and their covariance is a centered, rescaled inner product:

$$\mathrm{cov}(X,Y) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N (x_i-m(X))(y_i-m(Y))}{N} = \frac{c_X\cdot c_Y}{N}$$

Positive covariance means \(X\) tends to run above its own mean exactly when \(Y\) runs above its mean (and below together too); negative means they tend to move in opposite directions; zero means \(X\) carries no linear information about \(Y\), or vice versa.

Orthogonality

Two vectors are orthogonal exactly when \(X \cdot Y = 0\). This single equation recurs across the rest of this course and well beyond it — independence of random variables, the optimality conditions that pin down a regression's coefficients, unsupervised learning methods like PCA all reduce to some version of "these two things are orthogonal."

DS applications of the inner product

Once you see the pattern, it is everywhere: covariance is a centered dot product, correlation a normalized one, and independence implies zero covariance. Computing an expectation is a dot product of probabilities against values, \(\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{x} p(x)\,x = p\cdot X\). Solving for a linear regression's coefficients is the orthogonality condition \(X^\top(y - X\beta) = 0\); its predictions are \(\hat y_i = x_i \cdot \beta\). Comparing how similar two documents or images are — cosine similarity of embeddings — is a normalized dot product; word2vec and attention are scaled dot products measuring token relevance; deep learning alternates stacked dot products with non-linear activations to build up complex learned functions. GPUs are worth what they're worth because they were built to compute dot products as fast as physically possible — a large part of why Nvidia is a trillion-dollar company traces back to this one operation. Translating an ML idea into fast code is, very often, exactly the problem of writing it as a dot product.

Matrix multiplication as stacked dot products

Matrix multiplication is nothing but an efficient way to stack inner-product calculations: "row times column" is a dot product. That is why \(A \times B = C\) requires \(A\) to be \((N \times M)\) and \(B\) to be \((M \times L)\), producing an \((N \times L)\) result — every row of \(A\) has length \(M\), so every column of \(B\) must match that length for the dot product to make sense, and you get one result per (row, column) pair, \(N \times L\) of them.

Writing \(A\)'s \(r\)-th row as \(a_{r,:}\) and \(B\)'s \(c\)-th column as \(b_{:,c}\), matrix-times-vector computes the inner product of every row of \(A\) with \(x\):

$$Ax = \left[\begin{array}{c} a_{1,:}\cdot x \\ a_{2,:}\cdot x \\ \vdots \\ a_{n,:}\cdot x \end{array}\right]$$

— A @ x or np.matmul(A, x), never A * x (that would be the Hadamard product, and it requires matching shapes rather than a shared inner dimension). Matrix-times-matrix is the same idea applied to every column of \(B\) in turn:

$$AB = \left[\begin{array}{ccc} a_{1,:}\cdot b_{:,1} & \cdots & a_{1,:}\cdot b_{:,\ell} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ a_{n,:}\cdot b_{:,1} & \cdots & a_{n,:}\cdot b_{:,\ell} \end{array}\right]$$

Entry \((r,c)\) of \(AB\) is row \(r\) of \(A\) dotted with column \(c\) of \(B\) — nothing more exotic than that, repeated \(n \times \ell\) times. The Class 02 lab's closing section ties this back to the broadcasting-based distance matrix from Part 1: the same "grid of paired calculations" idea, computed two different ways.

FAQ

Is x.reshape(1,-1) the same as the transpose?

For a 1-D NumPy array, reshaping to a row or column is how you give it the row/column distinction at all — NumPy's native 1-D arrays don't track "row" versus "column" until you reshape (or transpose an already-2-D array) explicitly.

Why does covariance divide by N?

So that it is an average paired deviation, not a raw sum that mechanically grows just because you added more observations — the same reason the sample mean divides by n instead of reporting the bare total.

Why is matrix multiplication not the same as A * B?

A * B multiplies matching entries with no summation and needs identical shapes; A @ B sums a full dot product per (row, column) pair and needs only a shared inner dimension. They are different operations serving different purposes, not two notations for the same thing.
