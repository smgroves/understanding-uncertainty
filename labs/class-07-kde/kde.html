Kernel Density Estimation

Drawing a smooth curve where a histogram draws bars
Understanding Uncertainty · a build-it-from-scratch walkthrough

A histogram answers "how many cars cost between $5k and $6k?" But the choice of bins is arbitrary, and the answer jumps as you slide the edges. The kernel density estimator asks a smoother question — "how densely are prices packed right around $5k?" — and answers it with one tunable knob instead of a grid of bins.

Built on the Class 07 notebooks 05_1_kde and 05_2_gaussian_kernel. Data: 92 used-car listings scraped from Charlottesville Craigslist (cville_cars.csv), prices in thousands of dollars.

Estimating a density

The density \(f(x) = F'(x)\) is the rate of change of the CDF. Intuitively, it measures the probability that \(X\) lands in a tiny neighbourhood around \(x\). The sample version of that question is concrete: what proportion of observations fall in a window from \(x-h\) to \(x+h\)?

Slide the window below along the price axis. The count of cars inside the shaded band, divided by the total and by the window width, is your first density estimate — a "moving-window histogram" that no longer depends on where the bin edges happen to sit.

Moving window · counting is estimating
Center a window at \(x\) with half-width \(h\). Count the points inside, then normalize. Watch what the estimate does when the window is very narrow versus very wide.

That recipe is the whole idea. The count of points with \(|x - x_i| < h\), divided by \(2nh\), is a legitimate estimate of the density at \(x\). The next section shows it is exactly the slope of the ECDF — which is what a density is.

The uniform kernel

Recall the ECDF \(\hat{F}(x)\): the staircase that climbs by \(1/n\) at each data point and reports the proportion of the sample at or below \(x\). A density is a derivative of a CDF, so estimate the density by taking a secant of the ECDF across a window of width \(2h\):

$$\hat{f}_{X,h}(x) = \frac{\hat{F}_X(x+h) - \hat{F}_X(x-h)}{2h} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{I}\{\,|x - x_i| < h\,\}}{2nh}$$

The numerator is an indicator sum: it counts the points inside the window, exactly what you did by hand above. Written this way, the estimator is a sum of one identical function centered on each data point — the uniform kernel:

$$k_u(z) = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbb{I}\{\,|z| < 1\,\}, \qquad \hat{f}_{X,h}(x) = \frac{1}{nh}\sum_{i=1}^n k_u\!\left(\frac{x - x_i}{h}\right)$$

In code the whole estimator is four lines. The one subtlety is step 3, where broadcasting compares every grid point against every data point at once:

def kde_uniform(X, K=120):
    n   = len(X)
    h   = 1.84 * X.std() * n ** (-0.2)          # Silverman, uniform kernel
    xg  = np.linspace(X.min() - h, X.max() + h, K)
    I   = np.abs(xg[:, None] - X[None, :]) < h  # (K x n) window membership
    return xg, I.sum(axis=1) / (2 * n * h)      # proportion, per unit width
Check your understanding

In the playground at the top, switch to the uniform kernel and drag the bandwidth to its smallest value. Why does the curve turn into a picket fence of narrow spikes?

Show answer

As \(h \to 0\) each window is so narrow it contains only the point(s) exactly at its center, and the \(1/(2nh)\) normalizer blows up. You get a spike of height \(\propto 1/h\) at every observation and zero between them — the "comb" that a rug plot draws. The estimator is unbiased in the limit but useless: all variance, no smoothing.

Here is a third way to read the same formula, the measurement-error view. Imagine each price \(x_i\) was measured with a bit of slop, so it is really spread over a small bump of its own. Each point contributes \(1/n\) of a unit of area:

$$\hat{f}_{n,h}(x) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{h}\,k\!\left(\frac{x - x_i}{h}\right)$$

Below is a hand-traceable sample of just five prices, \(X = \{2, 4, 5, 5, 9\}\) (in thousands). Each faint green curve is one point's bump; the bold curve is their average. Notice the double-height contribution where the two 5s coincide — that is the tallest part of the estimate.

Each data point gets an identical kernel scaled to area \(1/n\). Sum them and you have the KDE. Toggle between the box (uniform) and bell (Gaussian) bump, and change the bandwidth to widen or narrow every bump at once.

Three views, one formula

The moving window, the slope of the ECDF, and the stack of measurement-error bumps are not three estimators — they are three readings of the same sum \(\frac{1}{nh}\sum_i k\!\left(\frac{x-x_i}{h}\right)\). Whichever picture you find intuitive, the arithmetic is identical.

The Gaussian kernel

The uniform kernel gives a jagged estimate: as the window slides, points pop in and out, so the curve has flat steps and sharp jumps. Swap the box for a smooth bump and those jumps disappear. The standard choice is the Gaussian kernel, the bell curve itself:

$$\phi(z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\!\left(\frac{-z^2}{2}\right)$$

It is non-negative, it is maximized at \(z=0\), it decays quickly as \(|z|\) grows, and it integrates to 1 — everything a kernel needs. Because a point's influence now fades gradually instead of cutting off at the window edge, a point near \(x\) counts more than one far away, and the estimate is smooth. Only the kernel changes; the formula \(\frac{1}{nh}\sum_i k(\frac{x-x_i}{h})\) does not.

Note · same knob, different constant

Changing the kernel changes the best default bandwidth. Silverman's rule uses \(h = 1.84\,\mathrm{sd}(X)\,n^{-1/5}\) for the uniform kernel, but the robust Gaussian form is \(h = 0.9\,\min\{\mathrm{sd}(X),\, \mathrm{IQR}/1.34\}\,n^{-1/5}\). Press the Silverman's h button in the playground with each kernel selected to see the two values.

Choosing the bandwidth

The bandwidth is the bias–variance trade-off in one slider. Too small and the estimate is all variance — it chases noise and shows clusters that are not there. Too large and it is all bias — it smooths real structure into a featureless hill. Click through the three regimes:

Under-, well-, and over-smoothed
The same 92 prices under three bandwidths. The used-car market really has two clusters — cheap beaters near $3k and pricier trucks past $15k. Only the middle bandwidth shows both honestly.

Silverman's rule is a plug-in starting point, not the final word. Because its bandwidth shrinks as \(n\) grows (\(h \propto n^{-1/5}\)), the KDE is a consistent but biased estimator: at any finite sample it is a little off, but as you collect more cars the bias vanishes and the curve converges to the true density. That is usually the trade you want — a small, disappearing bias in exchange for a smooth, readable estimate.

FAQ

Is a KDE just a smooth histogram?

Essentially yes, with two upgrades: it does not depend on where bin edges fall, and (with a smooth kernel) it produces a differentiable curve. The bandwidth plays the role of bin width.

Why does the estimate not quite reach area 1 on the plot?

The estimate itself is never negative and integrates to 1 in theory. The tails simply extend a little past the data by \(\pm h\), which is why the plotting grid starts at X.min() − h. Numerically the area is very close to 1; small deficits come from truncating those tails on a finite grid.

Does the kernel choice matter?

Far less than the bandwidth. Gaussian versus uniform mainly changes smoothness at the edges. Getting \(h\) right is the whole game — which is why the bandwidth section is the one to linger on.

How would I pick \(h\) without Silverman?

Least-squares cross-validation: hold out points, score how well the estimate predicts them, and pick the \(h\) that scores best. Silverman is the fast default; cross-validation is the principled-but-slower alternative.
