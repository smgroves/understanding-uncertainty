The Bootstrap

Simulating the sampling distribution from the one sample you have
Understanding Uncertainty · a build-it-from-scratch walkthrough

You measured the average used-car price and got $8.59k. Run the survey again next week and you would get a different number — but you can't run it again, this is all the data you have. The bootstrap answers "how much would that statistic wobble across repeated samples?" using nothing but resampling: treat your one sample as the population, draw from it, and watch a point estimate turn into a range.

Built on the Class 10 notebook 09_2_intro_to_bootstrap (and its Class 08 lead-in on sampling distributions). Data: 92 used-car listings scraped from Charlottesville Craigslist (cville_cars.csv), prices in thousands of dollars — the same sample as the Class 07 KDE lab.

The distribution you can't see

A statistic is random because your sample is random. Write the statistic as \(\hat{S} = S(X_1,\dots,X_n)\) — the mean, say. Draw a fresh sample and you get a slightly different \(\hat{S}\). The distribution of \(\hat{S}\) over all the samples you could have drawn is its sampling distribution, and its spread is exactly the uncertainty you want to report.

$$\hat{S} = S(X_1, \dots, X_n), \qquad X_i \sim F \;\text{(the true, unknown population)}$$

In Class 08 you approximated this distribution by simulating the experiment many times — and got those suspiciously bell-shaped curves. But that cheat needs the population \(F\), or the ability to re-run the experiment. In real work you have neither. You have one sample, drawn once. The bootstrap is the way out.

Treating the sample as the population

  • Share · 2 minAgree on one sentence explaining why replacement is non-negotiable, then check it against the mechanics below.

The bootstrap's single idea: since your data were drawn from the true generating process, resample the data to imitate that process. Sampling from your sample is the closest thing to sampling from \(F\) that you can actually do. One bootstrap resample \(D^*\) is \(n\) draws from \(\{x_1,\dots,x_n\}\), each draw independent and uniform over the sample — with replacement:

$$D^* = (x_{i_1}, \dots, x_{i_n}), \qquad i_j \sim \mathrm{Uniform}\{1,\dots,n\}\ \text{i.i.d.}$$

Replacement is the whole point. Draw without replacement and you would just get a reshuffle of the original 92 — the mean would be identical every single time, and there would be no wobble to measure. With replacement, each resample has a different makeup: that is what you saw in the widget when one listing appeared four times and dozens were left out.

The resample in one line of code

An index into the sample is int(rng() · n); a resample is n of them: [X[int(rng()*n)] for _ in range(n)]. That single line is the engine of everything below, and it is the first function you will write in the assignment.

Building the bootstrap distribution

Fix a number of resamples \(B\). For each \(t = 1,\dots,B\), draw a resample and recompute the statistic, \(\hat{S}_t = S(D^*_t)\). The collection of those \(B\) numbers is a stand-in for the sampling distribution you could never observe directly:

$$\{\hat{S}_1, \dots, \hat{S}_B\} \;\approx\; \text{sampling distribution of } \hat{S}$$

Press Play and watch the histogram of \(\hat{S}_t\) accumulate. Toggle between the mean and the median. The dashed lines mark the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles; the shaded band between them is the 95% interval; the solid teal line is the observed statistic on the full sample.

Bootstrap distribution builder · press Play
Each bar counts how many of the \(B\) resamples produced a statistic in that slice. The cloud is centred on the observed value and far narrower than the raw price spread — because averaging 92 prices smooths out the extremes.
With \(B = 2000\) and seed 42 this is the exact distribution sampler.py ci prints — same seeded generator, same numbers: a 95% CI of about (6.95, 10.21) for the mean.
Note · what \(B\) controls, and what it doesn't

More resamples make the histogram smoother and the percentiles steadier, but they do not shrink the interval — \(B\) is your simulation budget, not your data. The width is set by the sample (its size \(n\) and its spread), which is the subject of the last section.

The percentile confidence interval

Think · Pair · Share · reading an interval off a histogram
  • Think · 2 minYou have 2000 bootstrap means in a list. You want an interval that captures the middle 95% of them. Predict the recipe — which two numbers from the list are the endpoints, and how would you find them without assuming any bell curve?
I've done the Think step — reveal Pair & Share
  • Pair · 3 minCompare recipes. Did you both reach "sort the list, chop 2.5% off each end"? That is all a percentile interval is.
  • Share · 2 minAgree on which percentiles a 90% interval would use instead, then check the formula below.

A confidence interval is a range of plausible values for the statistic. The percentile bootstrap builds it with no normal-curve assumption at all: sort the bootstrap statistics and read off two percentiles. For a level \(\alpha\), the \((1-\alpha)\) interval is the \(\alpha/2\) and \(1-\alpha/2\) quantiles of the collection:

$$\mathrm{CI}_{1-\alpha} = \left(q_{\alpha/2},\; q_{1-\alpha/2}\right), \qquad 95\%:\ \left(q_{0.025},\; q_{0.975}\right)$$

So the 95% interval is the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles, the 90% interval is the 5th and 95th, and the 99% interval is the 0.5th and 99.5th. On this data the mean's 95% interval is about (6.95, 10.21) $k and the median's is about (4.24, 7.00) $k — both comfortably bracketing their observed values (8.59 and 5.65). Reporting the interval instead of the bare point estimate is the honest move: it says how much the number could have wobbled.

Check your understanding

The median's bootstrap interval has visibly flat, blocky edges in the widget, while the mean's is smooth. Why does the median's bootstrap distribution look so much chunkier?

Show answer

The median of 92 numbers is always the average of two of the actual observed prices, so it can only land on a small menu of discrete values (5.0, 5.15, 5.3, …). Many resamples produce the exact same median, giving tall, discrete spikes rather than a smooth bell. The mean, by contrast, is an average of 92 values and moves near-continuously, so its distribution is smooth.

Standard error and the role of n

Think · Pair · Share · what makes an interval tight
  • Think · 2 minYou want a narrower confidence interval. You have two knobs: collect more real listings (grow \(n\)), or run more resamples (grow \(B\)). Predict which one narrows the interval, which one doesn't, and why. Roughly how much smaller does the interval get if you quadruple \(n\)?
I've done the Think step — reveal Pair & Share
  • Pair · 3 minCompare with a neighbour: which knob is "more data" and which is "more computing"? Only one buys you precision.
  • Share · 2 minCommit to a prediction for the \(n\)-vs-\(B\) question, then test both sliders in the widget.

The standard error is the spread of the sampling distribution, so the bootstrap estimates it for free: it is just the standard deviation of the bootstrap statistics. For the mean, that bootstrap SE lands right on the textbook formula \(\mathrm{sd}(X)/\sqrt{n}\):

$$\widehat{\mathrm{SE}} = \mathrm{sd}\{\hat{S}_1,\dots,\hat{S}_B\}, \qquad \mathrm{SE}(\bar{X}) = \frac{\mathrm{sd}(X)}{\sqrt{n}}$$

Drag the two sliders below. Growing \(n\) tightens the interval — the \(\sqrt{n}\) in the denominator means quadrupling your listings halves the width. Growing \(B\) barely moves the width; it only steadies the estimate. That is the lesson: precision is bought with data, not with simulation.

Standard error · grow n, not B
The accent whisker is the bootstrap 95% CI; the teal whisker is the classical interval mean ± 1.96 · sd/√n. They nearly coincide — the bootstrap rediscovers the standard-error formula without being told it.

The two intervals sitting almost on top of each other is the payoff. The bootstrap did not know the \(\mathrm{sd}/\sqrt{n}\) rule; it just resampled and measured a spread — and recovered the same answer. When a statistic has no tidy standard-error formula (a median, a correlation, a ratio), the bootstrap still works exactly the same way, which is why it is the go-to tool for uncertainty in practice.

FAQ

Why with replacement? Why not just shuffle?

A shuffle (draw without replacement) returns the same 92 numbers in a new order, so every "resample" has the identical mean — zero wobble, nothing to measure. Replacement lets each resample have a genuinely different composition, which is what generates the spread the bootstrap reads as uncertainty.

Does the confidence interval say "95% chance the true mean is inside"?

Not quite. The frequentist reading is that if you repeated the whole procedure many times, about 95% of the intervals produced would contain the true value. The percentile bootstrap is an approximation to that guarantee — good for roughly symmetric statistics, less exact for very skewed ones or tiny samples.

How large should B be?

Large enough that the interval stops moving when you re-run with a new seed — typically 1000–10000. Bigger \(B\) buys a smoother, steadier estimate of the interval, never a narrower one. If you want a narrower interval, you need more data, not more resamples.

Can I bootstrap any statistic?

Yes — that is the appeal. The recipe never mentions the mean specifically; swap in a median, a trimmed mean, a correlation, an R², or a model's test accuracy and the machinery is unchanged. It shines exactly where no clean standard-error formula exists.
