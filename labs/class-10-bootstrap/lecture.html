The Bootstrap

Simulating the sampling distribution you can never observe, from the one sample you have
Understanding Uncertainty · lecture notes, digitized

You cannot re-run the world to see how much a statistic would wobble across repeated samples — you are a data scientist, and the data in front of you are usually all the data there will ever be. The bootstrap's answer is almost cheeky: treat your one sample as if it were the entire population, resample from it with replacement, and use the spread of the results as a stand-in for the sampling distribution you could never actually observe.

Part 1 · Simulating Sampling Distributions

A statistic is itself a random variable

Take any statistic \(S\) computed on a sample, \(S(X_1,\dots,X_n)\) — a mean, a median, a correlation, anything. Draw a different sample and you get a different number back, so \(S\) is a function of random inputs and is therefore itself a random variable, with its own distribution. Repeat the draw-and-compute step many times and the resulting values of \(S\) pile up into an ECDF — an estimate of the distribution that \(S\) itself follows. That distribution is the sampling distribution: not the distribution of the data, but the distribution of a number computed from the data.

The simulation matrix

Make this concrete by drawing \(N\) values of an iid random variable \(X\), and doing that whole draw \(T\) separate times:

$$
\left[
    \begin{array}{cccc}
X_{11} & X_{12} & \dots & X_{1T} \\
X_{21} & X_{22} & \dots & X_{2T} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
X_{N1} & X_{N2} & \dots & X_{NT} \\
    \end{array}
    \right]
$$

Each column is one simulated sample; each row is the position of a draw within its sample, so \(X_{it}\) is the \(i\)-th draw of the \(t\)-th simulation. Growing the number of rows grows the sample size \(N\); growing the number of columns grows the number of simulated repetitions \(T\). The question this whole class is building toward: as \(T\to\infty\), what does the distribution of \(S_t = S(X_{1t},\dots,X_{Nt})\) look like, for a fixed sample size \(N\)?

The frequentist thought experiment

For the sample mean specifically, the answer is already in hand from earlier classes: \(\mathbb{E}[\bar X_N] = \mathbb{E}[X]\), \(\mathbb{V}[\bar X_N] = \mathbb{V}[X]/N\), and \(pr[|\bar X_N - \mathbb{E}[X]| > d] \le \mathbb{V}[X]/Nd^2\) by Chebyshev. Each of those facts describes one column of the matrix above, for a fixed \(N\); replicating the study many times is exactly what growing \(T\) means. This is the foundational thought experiment of frequentist statistics: if I repeated this entire analysis many times, what would typically happen? The sample mean has a closed-form answer to that question. Most statistics of interest — a median, a correlation, an odds ratio — do not. Simulation is how you answer the same question for those, by literally building the \(T\)-column matrix and watching what \(S_t\) does. Part 2 replaces even the simulation step: instead of drawing fresh columns from a generating process you don't have direct access to, you resample the one column of real data you actually collected.

Part 2 · The Bootstrap

The bootstrap idea

Previously, a statistic's sampling distribution was built by simulating an experiment many times and recomputing the statistic each time — a series of suspiciously bell-shaped curves. In practice you usually cannot run the experiment again: the data you have are often all the data there will ever be.

The bootstrap: since your one sample was drawn from the true generating process, simply resample it to simulate that process — treat the sample as a stand-in for the population it came from.

Sampling with replacement

The resampling step draws \(n\) observations from your existing \(n\)-point sample, each draw independent and free to land on any observation — so a single point may be picked several times in one resample and not at all in another. That reuse is exactly what makes each resample differ from the original sample, and from every other resample; a resample drawn without replacement would just be a reordering of the same \(n\) numbers, with zero variation to measure.

Bootstrapping the sampling distribution

Fix a number of bootstrap replications \(B\). For \(t = 1,\dots,B\): draw a resample of \(n\) observations with replacement, \(D_t\), and compute the statistic on it, \(\hat S_t = S(D_t)\). The collection \([\hat S_1,\dots,\hat S_B]\) approximates the sampling distribution of \(\hat S\).

From that collection alone: the ECDF estimates the sampling distribution itself; the KDE estimates its density; and the standard deviation of \([\hat S_1,\dots,\hat S_B]\) is the standard error of \(\hat S\) — three different summaries of one collection of numbers.

Bootstrapping versus simulation

Line the bootstrap replicates up in the same matrix shape as Part 1's simulation matrix, to see exactly what changed:

$$
\left[
    \begin{array}{cccc}
X_{11} & X_{12} & \dots & X_{1B} \\
X_{21} & X_{22} & \dots & X_{2B} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
X_{N1} & X_{N2} & \dots & X_{NB} \\
    \end{array}
    \right]
$$

Each column is one bootstrap resample, drawn with replacement and the same size \(N\) as the real dataset; each row is the position of a draw within its resample, so \(X_{ib}\) is the \(i\)-th draw of the \(b\)-th resample. The sample size \(N\) is now fixed by the data you actually collected, not a free choice. As the number of columns \(B\) grows large, the ECDF of a statistic computed across those columns becomes an estimate of its sampling distribution — the same target as Part 1's simulation matrix, reached by resampling data instead of drawing fresh simulations from a generating process you don't have.

The variation already inside your data

It can feel like resampling the same \(N\) rows over and over must eventually run out of new information. It does not, for any dataset of realistic size. There are \(N\) choices for the first draw of a resample, \(N\) choices for the second, and so on, for \(N\) draws — so there are \(N^N\) distinct ways to build one bootstrap resample. At \(N=100\), \(100^{100}\) dwarfs the roughly \(10^{80}\) atoms in the observable universe, the \(10^{11}\) stars in an average galaxy, and even the \(10^{12}\) galaxies in the observable universe. No computer will ever exhaust the ways of resampling even a moderately sized dataset, which is exactly why a few thousand bootstrap replicates already capture real variation rather than repeating the same handful of resamples.

Communicating results: the confidence interval

The full distribution of replicates is an honest, complete answer, but people often want a simpler numeric summary. The usual choice: report an interval \((L,H)\) that captures 90/95/99% of the replicates, as a bound on the values this kind of process tends to produce. This is a confidence interval.

Three levels come up constantly: the 90% interval is the 0.05 and 0.95 quantiles of \([\hat S_1,\dots,\hat S_B]\); the 95% interval is the 0.025 and 0.975 quantiles; the 99% interval is the 0.005 and 0.995 quantiles. In general, for level parameter \(\alpha\) (typically 0.10, 0.05, or 0.01), the non-parametric \((1-\alpha)\)-confidence interval is built in two steps: find the \(\alpha/2\) and \(1-\alpha/2\) quantiles of the replicate collection, \(q_{\alpha/2}\) and \(q_{1-\alpha/2}\), and report \((q_{\alpha/2},\, q_{1-\alpha/2})\).

Hypothesis testing

Suppose there is a hypothesized value you particularly care about — often zero, but it could be anything. Asking "is my hypothesized value inside the confidence interval?" is exactly a statistical test: the null hypothesis is that the hypothesized value describes reality (\(S=0\), say); the alternative hypothesis is that it doesn't (\(S \neq 0\)). The tricky part, psychologically, is that researchers usually want to reject the null and find evidence for the alternative — but the test itself doesn't care which outcome you're hoping for.

Interpreting the bootstrap and the CI

A 95% confidence interval means, literally: "if you repeated this entire procedure many times, 95% of the resulting intervals would contain the true value of \(S\)." It does not mean "there is a 95% probability the true value of \(S\) is in this particular interval" — the true value either is or is not in any one interval, full stop, with no probability attached to it once it's drawn. What the confidence level actually quantifies is how much sampling variation exists in your measurement procedure. A confidence interval is a statement about the reliability of a procedure, not a truth machine that tells you where the truth is.

FAQ

Why does resampling need replacement specifically?

Without replacement, a "resample" of size \(n\) from an \(n\)-point sample is just the original sample shuffled into a new order — every such resample has the exact same statistic as the original, so there is nothing to measure. Replacement is what lets each resample's composition genuinely differ, which is the entire source of the spread the bootstrap reads as uncertainty.

Does a wider confidence interval mean a worse experiment?

Not necessarily — it means more sampling variation in that specific statistic, which can come from a smaller sample size, a more variable underlying population, or a statistic that is inherently noisier to estimate (a correlation typically has more sampling variation than a mean, for instance). A wide interval is honest information, not necessarily a flaw.

Can hypothesis testing and confidence intervals disagree?

No — by construction they are the same test read two ways. Rejecting the null at level \(\alpha\) is defined as the hypothesized value falling outside the \((1-\alpha)\) confidence interval; the two procedures cannot give contradictory answers because one is just a restatement of the other.
